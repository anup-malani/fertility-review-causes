# Chapter Writing Style Guide

This guide records reader-facing writing rules that emerged from the smart-undergrad review of the
old-age-security chapter. Apply these rules to the whole chapter, not only to the paragraph where a
comment was first made.

## Audience

Write for an intelligent reader who is not working inside the review pipeline. The reader may know
basic economics, but should not need project labels, extraction-sheet vocabulary, or internal review
history to follow the argument.

## Core Rules

1. Be explicit about the argument being evaluated.
   Do not write vague setup sentences such as "the pattern is mixed" or "this raises a different
   question" unless the sentence immediately states what pattern or question is meant.

2. Prefer direct claims over cryptic technical phrasing.
   Avoid phrases such as "causal contrast" unless the contrast is named in plain English. Say what is
   being compared and what the evidence implies.

3. Avoid "yeah, but" structure unless the second clause adds real information.
   If a sentence says the evidence supports one version of the argument but not another, name both
   versions directly.

4. Use formal, parallel descriptions.
   Do not put unlike objects in parallel. For example, write "long-term-care insurance in China,
   social insurance in Bismarckian Prussia, and U.S. Social Security during the baby boom" rather than
   mixing policy types with places or shorthand labels.

5. Avoid unexplained internal labels.
   Do not use labels such as "Cell A" or "Cell C" before defining them. In reader-facing prose, prefer
   explicit names such as "primary old-age-security evidence" or "grandparental childcare evidence."

6. Avoid using "sign" for effects.
   Say whether the evidence implies an increase or decrease in fertility. Use "positive" or
   "negative" only when discussing coefficient values.

7. Define vocabulary before abbreviating it.
   Write out terms such as "pay-as-you-go pension system" before using abbreviations such as "PAYG."
   Common methods abbreviations such as DiD can be used when already familiar in context.

8. Keep lists short.
   Use no more than three examples unless a longer list is essential. If more than three items are
   required, group them into categories rather than listing every item.

9. Avoid colon-led list sentences in explanatory prose.
   Do not write a sentence that introduces a long list after a colon. Turn it into a sentence with one
   to three examples, or split it into multiple sentences.

10. Use examples to clarify, not to overwhelm.
    When examples are needed, choose the three most representative ones. Do not use examples as a
    substitute for a clear general statement.

11. Make transitions self-contained.
    At the start of a paragraph, do not rely on "that," "this," or "the pattern" unless the noun is
    repeated. A reader should not need to remember the previous paragraph's grammatical object.

12. Describe mechanisms in ordinary causal language.
    For the grandparental childcare channel, prefer "childcare cost reduction" or "grandparents'
    childcare availability" over abstract phrases such as "time transfer."

13. Keep methods and workflow details out of the main argument.
    Search logs, extraction files, approval status, and variable-coding details belong in the
    reproducibility appendix or project documentation, not in the reader-facing chapter body.

14. Avoid over-hyphenated modifier chains.
    If a phrase reads like several nouns compressed into one adjective, rewrite it as an ordinary
    noun phrase. For example, prefer "evidence for using children as pensions" or "grandparental
    childcare evidence" over long stacked modifiers.

15. Avoid unnecessary "not ... but ..." constructions.
    Use contrast only when the reader might reasonably expect the rejected claim. Otherwise, state the
    affirmative point directly and explain the implication.

16. Avoid generic adjectives for mechanisms.
    Do not call a mechanism "classic," "standard," or "main" when the sentence can name the actual
    mechanism. Prefer "children support parents in old age" or "non-child old-age security replaces
    children" to vague labels such as "classic motive."

17. Do not make readers remember table rows.
    In prose, refer to studies by author and setting rather than by row counts or table positions.
    Write "Ilciukas's Netherlands estimates and Akyol and Atalay's Australia estimates" instead of
    "six rows" when the identity of the evidence matters.

## Examples

Weak:

> The Second Demographic Transition raises a different question.

Better:

> The Second Demographic Transition asks whether pension policy still matters once public pensions
> and private savings are already widespread.

Weak:

> This gives the chapter its primary causal contrast.

Better:

> The comparison is between families that gain a non-child source of old-age support and otherwise
> similar families that do not.

Weak:

> The sign is opposite in Cell C.

Better:

> The grandparental childcare channel predicts that more grandparent availability raises fertility,
> whereas replacing children with non-child old-age security predicts lower fertility.
