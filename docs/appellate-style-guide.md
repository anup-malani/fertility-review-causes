# Appellate Prose Style Guide

> **For the fertility-review team.** This is our shared writing style guide, adopted for the systematic-review project. The aim is prose that is brutally simple and clear: lead with the point, say it in plain concrete words, vary the rhythm, and cut every word that does no work. Apply it as a revision pass, after a draft's content and structure are settled. It sharpens sentences; it does not decide what to say. Where it mentions a "house style" or "voice," read that as whatever the target venue — a review chapter, a methods memo, a paper — calls for.

## When to read this

Use this during a revision pass on any long-form piece — after content, structure, and argument are settled, when the task is sentence-level polish rather than first drafting. Apply it once the target voice for the piece is already chosen: this guide sharpens sentences within that voice, it does not choose the voice.

A positive voice target for long-form writing, distilled from roughly 112,000 words of exemplary appellate advocacy: majority opinions and a dissent by Chief Justice John Roberts, opinions and concurrences by Judge Jeffrey Sutton, and merits briefs by Paul Clement — spanning judicial opinion, concurrence, dissent, and merits-stage briefing, so the underlying craft generalizes across genres rather than reflecting any single one. What unites these writers is a single stance: the reader owes you nothing, so you earn every sentence by front-loading the point, saying it in plain concrete words, and varying the rhythm so the prose reads like a person thinking, not a machine filling a template.

> **On the quotations.** Every GROUNDING below is copied verbatim from the corpus and tagged with its case, so you can verify each rule against the paragons rather than against generic advice. The quotations preserve the source's own punctuation, including em-dashes used for pacing — this guide does not adopt that punctuation itself (see "Conflicts" below). Read the quotes for their structure, not their pointing.

**This guide is a craft supplement, not a replacement, for whatever house style governs your document.** Treat it as a rubric to layer on top of your target venue's voice during revision, not as a competing voice. Where an appellate habit collides with a rule your house style already enforces, the house style wins — the most common collision points are flagged below under "Conflicts to reconcile with your own house style," and a few habits that should never transfer from legal writing at all are flagged under "Do NOT transfer."

Treat the guide as a rubric, not an essay: read it in ten minutes, then apply the CHECK lines paragraph by paragraph.

## A. Openings and structure

**A1. Open by stating the whole argument in the first two or three sentences.**
Why: a reader who reads only your opening should already know what you claim and why it matters, so the rest of the piece confirms rather than suspends.
GROUNDING: *"These two cases raise a common question: whether the police may, without a warrant, search digital information on a cell phone seized from an individual who has been arrested."* (Riley v. California)
BEFORE: "This memo is organized as follows. Section 1 provides background, Section 2 discusses options, and Section 3 offers a recommendation."
AFTER: "Enrollment in the pilot program has fallen 40 percent since the fee waiver expired. I recommend reinstating the waiver for the next two quarters and tying it to an income cutoff, which the pilot's own data show reaches the same population at half the cost."
CHECK: Cover everything after the first three sentences and ask whether a stranger could state your claim. If not, the opening is throat-clearing.

**A2. Lead with the fact, and let the fact carry weight before you interpret it.**
Why: a concrete fact stated plainly recruits the reader's own judgment; an interpretation stated first asks for trust you have not earned.
GROUNDING: *"The Voting Rights Act of 1965 employed extraordinary measures to address an extraordinary problem."* (Shelby County v. Holder)
BEFORE: "It is well documented in the literature that price and adoption are closely related in these markets."
AFTER: "Raise the price of the device by ten percent and adoption falls by roughly six percent within a year. That elasticity holds across every market we examined."
CHECK: Does the paragraph's first sentence contain a fact a skeptic could check, or only a claim about the literature's mood? Rewrite if the latter.

**A3. Pose the real question in one plain sentence, then answer it.**
Why: naming the question in the reader's own words makes your answer legible and keeps you honest about what you are actually deciding.
GROUNDING: *"And all come down to the same question: Who decides?"* (DeBoer v. Snyder)
BEFORE: "The causes of urban decline in mid-century American cities remain a matter of considerable scholarly debate, touching on economic, racial, and political factors."
AFTER: "One question sits under all the others: why did people leave the cities that had just made them rich? Every account of highways and factories explains how they left, not why they wanted to."
CHECK: Can you point to a single sentence that states the question in under fifteen words? If the question is spread across a paragraph, it is not yet posed.

**A4. Open every paragraph with its conclusion.**
Why: a reader skimming only first sentences should follow the whole argument; the topic sentence is the paragraph's claim, not its setup.
GROUNDING: *"This led to an economic 'death spiral.'"* (King v. Burwell — the paragraph then explains the spiral, having named it first.)
BEFORE: "Consider next the waiting list. Providers report delays. Delays discourage applicants. Applicants withdraw before approval. This is the attrition problem."
AFTER: "Applicants quit the waiting list before they ever get approved, and the list itself is why. Providers report delays of six weeks or more, and every week on the list costs the program another applicant who simply moves on."
CHECK: Read only the first sentence of each paragraph, end to end. If the argument does not hold as a chain, the topic sentences are misplaced.

**A5. Concede the other side's strongest point in its own terms, then answer it.**
Why: stating the opposing case at full strength before you meet it signals confidence and earns the reader's trust in your answer.
GROUNDING: *"Petitioners' arguments about the plain meaning of Section 36B are strong. But while the meaning of the phrase ... may seem plain 'when viewed in isolation,' such a reading turns out to be 'untenable in light of [the statute] as a whole.'"* (King v. Burwell)
BEFORE: "Some might argue that the decline reflects reporting changes, but this view has various problems we discuss below."
AFTER: "The reporting-change account is right that agencies changed how they logged cases, and it explains part of the early drop. Its trouble is the pattern after 2015: reporting rules stayed fixed for a decade, and the decline kept going."
CHECK: Before every rebuttal, is the opposing claim stated in a form its own author would accept? If your version is a straw figure, rebuild it first.

## B. Sentences and rhythm

**B1. Vary sentence length, and after a long sentence land a short one.**
Why: uniform sentence length is the surest way to make prose feel mechanical, and a short sentence after a long one is where an argument lands.
GROUNDING: *"History judges us in this area. We do not judge history."* (Bormuth v. County of Jackson) — and, at its most compressed, *"So it has."* (Thomas More Law Center v. Obama)
BEFORE: "The bridge could not be repaired in time for the winter shipping season, and this is because the replacement girders had been ordered from a supplier who could not deliver them for eleven months, which meant the city had already lost the contract it was trying to save."
AFTER: "The replacement girders were eleven months out. So the bridge could not open before winter. The contract the city was racing to save was already gone."
CHECK: Mark the word count of each sentence in a paragraph. If they cluster within a few words of one another, break the pattern.

**B2. Keep subject, verb, and object together and near the front.**
Why: a buried verb makes the reader hold the sentence in suspension; naming the actor and action early lets meaning arrive on time.
GROUNDING: *"Congress passed the Affordable Care Act to improve health insurance markets, not to destroy them."* (King v. Burwell)
BEFORE: "The redesign of the intake process, which touches five different offices and requires no new statutory authority, cuts the average approval time in half."
AFTER: "The redesigned intake process, touching five offices and needing no new statute, cuts average approval time in half."
CHECK: Underline the main verb. If more than about ten words separate the subject from it, move the verb forward.

**B3. Write in the active voice and name the actor.**
Why: the passive hides who did what, and naming the actor — a policy, an agency, a specific choice — is usually the point of the sentence.
GROUNDING: *"Five lawyers have closed the debate and enacted their own vision of marriage as a matter of constitutional law."* (Obergefell dissent)
BEFORE: "It is assumed that demand was shaped by the subsidy structure introduced in 2004."
AFTER: "The 2004 subsidy structure shaped demand. I take that as given here and defend it in Section 4."
CHECK: Grep the paragraph for "was", "were", "is assumed", "it is". For each, ask who acted; if the actor matters, name it.

**B4. Put one idea in each sentence; split a compound claim.**
Why: two claims joined by "and" or a semicolon usually hide a logical step that a period would expose and let the reader check.
GROUNDING: *"But cars and broccoli are no more purchased for their 'own sake' than health insurance. They are purchased to cover the need for transportation and food."* (NFIB v. Sebelius)
BEFORE: "The factory closed and this displaced eight hundred workers overnight, since the parent company had already relocated the tooling to a plant in another state before the announcement."
AFTER: "The factory closed overnight, and eight hundred workers lost their jobs. The parent company had quietly moved the tooling to another state months before it announced anything."
CHECK: Count the independent claims in each sentence. More than one, and the sentence is doing two jobs — give the second its own sentence.

**B5. Let a single short sentence land, but never write staccato runs.**
Why: one punchy sentence carries weight; a string of them reads as fragments for effect, which flattens rather than sharpens the point.
GROUNDING: *"At the same time, voting discrimination still exists; no one doubts that."* (Shelby County — one short, complete sentence, set among long ones.)
BEFORE: "Costs rose. A lot. Across regions. Enrollment did not. It held steady. This is the puzzle."
AFTER: "Costs rose across every region, sometimes sharply, and enrollment did not follow. It held steady. That gap between cost and enrollment is the puzzle."
CHECK: If three or more consecutive sentences run under about eight words, you have staccato — fold some back into their neighbors.

## C. Words

**C1. Choose plain, concrete words over Latinate abstraction.**
Why: a plain word the reader already owns lands faster than an abstract one they must decode, and precision does not require Latin.
GROUNDING: *"This was strong medicine, but Congress determined it was needed ..."* (Shelby County — "strong medicine" for an aggressive statutory remedy.)
BEFORE: "The intervention effectuated a substantial diminution in the requisite quantum of visits necessary to achieve equivalent outcomes."
AFTER: "The intervention meant patients needed fewer visits to get the same result."
CHECK: Circle every word with a Latin ending (-tion, -ment, -ance, -ity). For each, ask whether a shorter, older word says the same thing.

**C2. Reach for a concrete image or everyday example to carry an abstract point.**
Why: an image the reader can picture fixes an abstraction in memory and does the work that three sentences of definition cannot.
GROUNDING: *"... modern cell phones, which are now such a pervasive and insistent part of daily life that the proverbial visitor from Mars might conclude they were an important feature of human anatomy."* (Riley v. California)
BEFORE: "The policy is calibrated for a set of conditions that have since changed, producing a mismatch between the rule and the world it now governs."
AFTER: "The rule was built for a world that no longer exists, like a lock fitted to a door that was later replaced. It still turns. It just doesn't hold anything shut."
CHECK: For each abstract paragraph, is there one thing the reader can see? If the whole passage is conceptual, add a single concrete image — and only one.

**C3. Cut throat-clearing, intensifiers, and hedges; state the claim flat.**
Why: "clearly", "it is worth noting", "arguably" either add nothing or quietly retract the claim; confidence is shown by stating the thing and stopping.
GROUNDING: *"There is no denying, however, that the conditions that originally justified these measures no longer characterize voting in the covered jurisdictions."* (Shelby County — a strong claim stated without a single intensifier propping it up.)
BEFORE: "It is worth noting that cost is arguably one of the most important and clearly significant barriers to enrollment."
AFTER: "Cost is the largest barrier to enrollment, ahead of paperwork and eligibility rules combined."
CHECK: Grep for "clearly", "obviously", "arguably", "it is worth noting", "quite", "very", "rather". Delete each and confirm the sentence is stronger, not weaker.

**C4. Turn nominalizations back into verbs.**
Why: "make a determination" buries the action in a noun; "determine" puts it back where the reader can see it move.
GROUNDING: *"... premiums rose by 78 percent and the number of people enrolled fell by 25 percent."* (King v. Burwell — actions as verbs, not "there was an increase in premiums".)
BEFORE: "The model provides an explanation for and offers a characterization of the observed decline in participation."
AFTER: "The model explains why participation declined."
CHECK: Find noun phrases built on a hidden verb ("provides an explanation", "offers a characterization", "performs an analysis"). Replace each with the verb.

## D. Argument and stance

**D1. Make transitions carry logic, not decoration.**
Why: "moreover" and "furthermore" only pile sentences up; a real transition tells the reader what the next sentence does to the last one.
GROUNDING: *"But things change, sometimes quickly."* (DeBoer v. Snyder) — and, closing an argument, *"The upshot of all this is that ..."* (King v. Burwell).
BEFORE: "Furthermore, the migration account also faces difficulties. Moreover, it under-explains the timing."
AFTER: "The migration account describes the change but not its timing. To say families moved for opportunity only renames the puzzle; the question is why they moved in that decade, from those towns, all at once."
CHECK: For each "moreover / furthermore / additionally", ask what logical relation it marks. If the answer is "another point", replace it with a transition that states the relation, or start a clean new paragraph.

**D2. State your position; refuse the hedge that hides it.**
Why: a piece is read for what its author concluded, and a hedge that conceals the conclusion wastes the reader's time.
GROUNDING: *"While difficult cases will surely arise about when persuasion crosses the line into coercion, this is not one of them."* (Clement, Florida v. HHS) — concedes the hard general case, then commits flatly on the one at hand.
BEFORE: "It could be argued that outreach plays a role, though the evidence is mixed and other factors may also be relevant."
AFTER: "Outreach is the main driver. Eligibility rules shape how many people ultimately qualify, but they do not explain why so few who already qualify ever apply."
CHECK: For each hedged sentence, ask "what do I actually think?" If the sentence does not answer that, rewrite it so it does — or move the genuine uncertainty to a limitations paragraph where it belongs.

**D3. Be even-handed about your own case, and concede what it cannot do.**
Why: rigorous nonfiction prizes precision over persuasion; conceding your own account's limits in your own voice is more convincing than defending them, and it is the honest thing.
GROUNDING: *"To be sure, the English right was not unfettered."* (Clement, Bruen brief — the advocate concedes a point against his own side before showing why it does not sink him.)
BEFORE: "Our model fully accounts for the observed variation in outcomes across all sites and time periods."
AFTER: "The model reproduces the outcomes at the three sites we study, and it does no more than that. It treats the funding change as exogenous, and it says nothing about sites that adopted the program early, which I take up as a limitation in Section 6."
CHECK: Does the piece concede at least one thing its own account cannot do, stated plainly and not buried? If every sentence sells, the reader stops believing the sale.

**D4. Earn emphasis by position, not by italics or intensifiers.**
Why: the strongest place in a paragraph is a short sentence at its end; weight comes from placement, so italics and "extremely" are admissions that the placement failed.
GROUNDING: *"When a line offers no meaningful distinctions, it is a good time to ask whether the court should draw it."* (Bormuth — the paragraph's whole case, compressed into its last, shortest sentence.)
BEFORE: "This decline is a critically important and highly significant feature that standard accounts are completely unable to explain."
AFTER: "Every standard account stops at the recession's end. The decline that follows it is the part none of them reaches."
CHECK: Find each italicized word and each intensifier used for emphasis. Delete it and move the claim to the end of its paragraph in a short sentence. If it still needs the italics, the sentence is in the wrong place.

## E. Revision

**E1. Read it aloud, and rewrite whatever trips the tongue.**
Why: the ear catches what the eye skips — a buried verb, a clause too long to hold, a rhythm that never varies.
GROUNDING: *"History judges us in this area. We do not judge history."* (Bormuth — the balance and the reversal are audible; they were written for the ear.)
BEFORE: (a sentence that cannot be read in one breath without losing the subject)
AFTER: (the same content, broken at the point where your breath ran out, with the subject restored at the front of the second sentence)
CHECK: Read the paragraph aloud once. Every place you stumble, run out of breath, or lose the thread is a place to cut or split. Mark it and fix it.

**E2. On a second pass, cut every word that does no work.**
Why: a non-working word costs the reader attention and returns nothing; economy is what makes the paragons read fast.
GROUNDING: *"A complaint is not evidence. Briefs are not evidence."* (Bormuth — nothing in either sentence can be removed without losing meaning.)
BEFORE: "In order to be able to explain the patterns that we observe in the data, it is necessary for the analysis to incorporate a mechanism of the behavioral kind."
AFTER: "Explaining the data needs a behavioral mechanism."
CHECK: Delete each word in turn and ask whether the meaning survives. If it does, the word was not working — leave it out.

## Conflicts to reconcile with your own house style

- **Em-dashes for pacing.** Garner and all three writers use em-dashes freely to mark a beat ("This is a case about change—and how best to handle it"; "Our role is more confined—'to say what the law is'"). Many house styles ban em-dashes as rhetorical pauses. If yours does, keep the ban: use a comma, a semicolon, or two sentences. Keep a source's own em-dashes only in verbatim quotations.
- **Rhetorical questions.** Sutton and Roberts advance argument by asking ("Who decides?"; "Just who do we think we are?"). Use a rhetorical question at most once in a section, and only when the answer is genuinely the reader's to supply; default to stating the claim if your house style favors declarative prose throughout. Never stack them the way an advocate does.
- **Triads and parallel series.** The paragons like three-part parallels ("text, history, tradition, and precedent"). A parallel series is fine when each item carries distinct information; cut it when the parallelism is doing cadence rather than work, especially if your house style caps how many items belong in one sentence.
- **"We" vs. "I."** Opinions speak as "we"/"the Court". For solo-authored prose, keep "I" for your own claims and reserve "we" for reader-with-author moves ("we can see in Figure 1 that ..."); for coauthored or institutional prose, "we" throughout is the norm — follow whichever your house style already sets.

## Do NOT transfer from legal writing

These are habits of the genre, not of good prose. Rigorous nonfiction must stay even-handed about its own limitations and prize precision over persuasion where the two conflict; each of the following pulls the other way.

- **Party labels** ("Petitioner", "Respondent", "the Government"). Nonfiction has mechanisms, actors, and data, not adversaries. Name the actual thing.
- **String citations and citation signals** ("see", "cf.", "id.", "supra", "accord"). Use your own field's normal citation style; never let a signal do the argument's work.
- **Adversarial or one-sided spin.** The advocate's job is to win; yours is to be right. Do not characterize a rival account as weaker than it is — state it at full strength (A5, D3) and then show the specific point where it fails.
- **Contempt for a "wrong" side** ("five lawyers have closed the debate"; "it is a bit rich to say"). Prior work and rival accounts are colleagues' work, not opponents.
- **Rhetorical grandstanding** (perorations, "Just who do we think we are?", appeals to history and destiny). Moving in an opinion; out of place where the reader wants the result and the evidence.
- **Terms of art and Latin** ("de novo", "arguendo", "simpliciter", "the canon against surplusage"). Every field has its own jargon; use only what the argument needs, and gloss it.
- **"Question presented" framing.** The formal legal caption ("The question presented is whether ...") is a docket convention. State your question in plain prose (A3), not as a caption.
- **The advocate's certainty.** An advocate can assert a client wins "under any faithful reading"; you cannot assert your own account wins under any reading, because you must concede where it does not (D3).

## F. Worked example (illustration only)

A hypothetical passage in the numbered-theory-then-empirics register common to economics and policy writing, shown before and after one pass of this guide. The BEFORE sentence is illustrative, not a real quotation from either cited paper.

BEFORE (illustrative): *"Its premise, in Card and Krueger (1994) and a long line of work before them, could hardly be simpler: what a minimum-wage increase changes is the wage floor actually binding in a given labor market, not the statutory figure as written, so where the floor already sits below the market wage, employment does not fall, and the textbook prediction fails to hold."*

The sentence is sound but runs some sixty words in one breath, chaining four clauses with the main claim ("the premise is simple") announced before the reader can see it (tensions A4, B1, B2, E1).

AFTER (guide-conforming rewrite): *"The premise could hardly be simpler. What a minimum-wage increase changes is the wage floor that actually binds in a given labor market, not the statutory figure as written. So where the floor already sits below the market wage, employment does not fall. Card and Krueger (1994), and a long line of work before them, built an empirical program on exactly this."*

What changed and why: the claim leads (A4); the sixty-word chain breaks into four sentences of varied length, with a short one landing first (B1, B5); each sentence carries one idea (B4); the citation moves to the end so it supports the point rather than interrupts it (D1); and the whole thing can now be read aloud in single breaths (E1).
