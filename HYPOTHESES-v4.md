# Master Hypothesis List — v4

**Status:** v4 — outline structure and Cultural sub-sections added, 2026-06-14. Content unchanged from v3. Supersedes `HYPOTHESES-v3.md`.

Phenomena codes: PM = pre-modern, FDT = First Demographic Transition (~1870–1965), SDT = Second Demographic Transition (~1965–present).

**Organization:** Two tiers. *Proximate causes* (Section A) are mechanisms that beg the question of *why* — they do not explain the underlying demand for fewer children. *Root causes* (Sections B–D) are the underlying drivers. Section E contains theoretical frameworks that are not themselves testable hypotheses.

## A. Proximate Causes

### A.1. Child Mortality Decline (Replacement and Insurance Effects)
- **slug:** `child-mortality-decline-replacement`
- **claim:** Falling infant and child mortality reduces desired and realized fertility through reduced replacement births and reduced hoarding/insurance births, lowering TFR roughly in proportion to the gap between gross and net reproduction rates.
- **why:** When more children survive to adulthood, parents who want N surviving children need fewer births to reach that target. They also have less reason to hoard extra births as insurance. Both effects push down realized fertility once mortality drops, though there is typically a lag.
- **phenomena:** PM, FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Notestein 1945, Preston 1978, Schultz 1997, Galor and Weil 2000, Doepke 2005
- **cross-ref:** C.3.c (Old-Age Security — insurance motive); B (evolutionary preference for surviving offspring)
- **notes:** Canonical FDT mechanism; timing problems (mortality often falls before fertility) and weak macro identification. Doepke 2005 distinguishes replacement/hoarding/q-q channels.

### A.2. Modern Contraceptive Technology and Diffusion
- **slug:** `contraceptive-technology-diffusion`
- **claim:** Introduction and diffusion of cheap, effective, reversible contraception (the Pill, IUDs, modern sterilization) lowers the cost of fertility control and reduces unwanted births, closing the gap between desired and realized fertility.
- **why:** Cheap, effective, reversible methods dramatically lower the effort and uncertainty of avoiding a birth. Couples who already want fewer kids can now achieve that target. Realized fertility moves closer to desired fertility.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Westoff and Ryder 1977, Goldin and Katz 2002, Bailey 2006, Bongaarts 1978, Michael 1973
- **cross-ref:** C (cost of fertility control); B.1 (Evolutionary Sex Drive — contraception severs sex-reproduction link)
- **notes:** Micro-identified (Comstock laws, state-level Pill access); Pritchett 1994 contests macro significance. Proximate: explains how desired fertility is achieved, not why it fell.

### A.3. Diffusion and Social-Learning of Fertility Control
- **slug:** `diffusion-of-fertility-control`
- **claim:** Fertility decline spreads through linguistic, religious, and social networks via diffusion of information about and legitimation of birth control, independent of underlying socioeconomic conditions.
- **why:** Even holding economics constant, knowing that birth control exists and is socially acceptable must spread before people adopt it. The idea travels through shared language, religion, and social ties faster than through economic boundaries.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Coale and Watkins 1986, Cleland and Wilson 1987, Bongaarts and Watkins 1996, Rosero-Bixby and Casterline 1993
- **cross-ref:** D.1.a (Postmaterialism); D.1.b (Cultural Westernization)
- **notes:** Proximate: describes the spread mechanism, not what is being spread or why people adopt it. Princeton project's residual explanation.

### A.4. Induced Abortion Access and Legalization
- **slug:** `induced-abortion-access`
- **claim:** Legalization and access to safe induced abortion lowers TFR by removing unwanted pregnancies that would otherwise result in live births.
- **why:** Legal, safe abortion lets women end unwanted pregnancies. When access expands, some fraction of conceptions no longer convert into births, so TFR falls mechanically.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Levine et al. 1999, Pop-Eleches 2006, Bongaarts 1978, Westoff et al. 1981
- **cross-ref:** A.6 (stigma reduction enables use)
- **notes:** Crucial in Eastern Europe, Japan postwar, USSR; ambiguous for Western FDT (mostly illegal then). Proximate: bridges desired and realized fertility.

### A.5. Organized Family Planning Programs and Access
- **slug:** `family-planning-programs`
- **claim:** Government and NGO family-planning programs expanding contraceptive supply, information, and subsidies reduce unmet need and lower TFR by closing the gap between desired and realized fertility.
- **why:** Even where people want fewer kids, contraceptives can be expensive, far away, or surrounded by misinformation. Programs that expand supply close this unmet-need gap; births fall because supply-side frictions, not preferences, were the binding constraint.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Bongaarts et al. 1990, Pritchett 1994, Joshi and Schultz 2013, Miller 2010, Cleland et al. 2006
- **cross-ref:** --
- **notes:** Pritchett argues demand dominates; Matlab and Profamilia quasi-experiments show meaningful supply effects. Proximate: supply-side facilitation, not demand-side cause. Salient for LMIC FDT.

### A.6. Reduction in Stigma Around Contraception and Abortion
- **slug:** `stigma-reduction-contraception-abortion`
- **claim:** Cultural legitimation of contraception and abortion — independent of technological availability — lowers fertility by enabling latent demand for fertility control to be realized.
- **why:** Even when contraception is technically available, social disapproval prevents adoption. As cultural stigma fades, latent demand translates into actual use and births fall.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Cleland and Wilson 1987, Bongaarts and Watkins 1996, Goldin and Katz 2002, Lesthaeghe 1983
- **cross-ref:** D.1.a (Postmaterialism); D.1.b (Cultural Westernization)
- **notes:** Proximate: the norm change that enables contraceptive use; the root cultural cause lives in D. Cleland-Wilson ideational argument.

### A.7. Age at Marriage and Marriage Timing (European Marriage Pattern)
- **slug:** `marriage-timing-age-at-marriage`
- **claim:** Later female age at first marriage and higher proportions never marrying mechanically reduce exposure to childbearing and lower TFR, with cross-population variation in the Hajnal pattern explaining substantial PM and early-FDT fertility differences.
- **why:** Most births in pre-modern settings happened inside marriage, so years spent unmarried are years without exposure to pregnancy. Shifts in age at first marriage are a powerful lever on TFR even without contraception.
- **phenomena:** PM, FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Hajnal 1965, Coale 1973, Wrigley and Schofield 1981, De Moor and van Zanden 2010
- **cross-ref:** D.2.b (Marriage and Family Norms); C.7.a (Marriage Market)
- **notes:** Core PM mechanism (Malthusian preventive check); non-marital fertility rise complicates SDT mapping.

### A.8. Parity-Specific Stopping and Spacing Behavior
- **slug:** `parity-progression-stopping-behavior`
- **claim:** Adoption of deliberate parity-specific stopping (rather than spacing) within marriage is the proximate behavioral signature of the FDT, and its diffusion accounts for the bulk of marital fertility decline.
- **why:** The FDT is characterized by the spread of deliberate stopping at low parities — couples treating family size as a target to hit and then halt. Once this becomes the norm, average completed fertility drops sharply.
- **phenomena:** FDT
- **seminal:** Coale and Watkins 1986, Henry 1961, Knodel 1979, Cleland and Wilson 1987
- **cross-ref:** D.1.a (Postmaterialism); D.2.b (Marriage and Family Norms)
- **notes:** Princeton European Fertility Project; methodological backbone (m and M indices). Proximate behavioral outcome.

### A.9. Population Age Structure and Demographic Momentum
- **slug:** `population-age-structure-momentum`
- **claim:** Shifts in the age structure (share of women in peak reproductive ages) drive crude birth rates and period TFR independent of any change in age-specific fertility, generating mechanical fertility movements.
- **why:** The crude birth rate depends on how many women of childbearing age exist, not just on per-woman fertility. A population with a large cohort of 20–30-year-olds records many births even at low per-woman rates.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Keyfitz 1971, Bongaarts and Bulatao 1999, Lutz et al. 2003
- **cross-ref:** --
- **notes:** Largely accounting/measurement; matters for interpreting CBR and short-run TFR but not for completed cohort fertility.

### A.10. Sex Ratio Imbalance and Marriage Market Effects
- **slug:** `sex-ratio-marriage-market`
- **claim:** Skewed adult sex ratios (war, migration, sex-selective abortion) shift marriage rates and bargaining power, affecting marriage timing and marital fertility.
- **why:** If one sex is in short supply, marriage markets clear differently — some cannot find partners, marriage gets delayed, and bargaining power inside marriages shifts. Fertility responds to the imbalance even though individual desires are unchanged.
- **phenomena:** PM, FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Guttentag and Secord 1983, Angrist 2002, Abramitzky et al. 2011
- **cross-ref:** C.7.a (Marriage Market); D.2.c (Son Preference — one driver of imbalance)
- **notes:** Proximate: raises the question of why sex ratios are skewed. Mostly local/short-run shocks; no evidence of first-order effect on global TFR since sex ratios are near the natural rate in aggregate.

### A.11. Tempo Effects and Birth Postponement
- **slug:** `tempo-effects-birth-postponement`
- **claim:** Rising mean age at first birth depresses period TFR mechanically during the postponement transition, accounting for a meaningful share of measured low SDT fertility without an equivalent change in completed cohort fertility.
- **why:** If every woman delays her first birth by a few years, there is a stretch of calendar time when fewer births are recorded even if each cohort eventually has the same number. Once delay stabilizes, measured fertility partly rebounds.
- **phenomena:** SDT
- **seminal:** Bongaarts and Feeney 1998, Sobotka 2004, Goldstein et al. 2009, Lesthaeghe 2010, Goldin 2006
- **cross-ref:** C.2.e (Female Wage — driver of timing); C.5.a (Economic Uncertainty); D.2.a (Female Empowerment)
- **notes:** Distinguishes quantum from tempo; tempo-adjusted TFR often closer to replacement.

### A.12. Twinning Rates and Multiple Births
- **slug:** `twinning-multiple-births`
- **claim:** Variation in twinning rates (genetic, ART-induced) modestly affects TFR by raising live births per pregnancy, with ART-induced multiples partially offsetting postponement-driven SDT declines.
- **why:** Each pregnancy does not have to produce exactly one baby. Populations with higher twinning rates get a small boost to TFR. ART-induced multiples now partially offset the SDT trend.
- **phenomena:** PM, SDT
- **seminal:** Bulmer 1970, Pison and D'Addato 2006, Hoekstra et al. 2008
- **cross-ref:** B (biological variation in twinning)
- **notes:** Small magnitude; included for completeness. PM variation across ethnic groups (Yoruba) documented.

### A.13. Breastfeeding and Lactational Amenorrhea
- **slug:** `breastfeeding-lactational-amenorrhea`
- **claim:** Longer and more intensive breastfeeding suppresses ovulation via lactational amenorrhea, lengthens birth intervals, and lowers natural fertility; shortening of breastfeeding duration raises fertility.
- **why:** Intensive breastfeeding suppresses the hormones that drive ovulation, so a nursing mother is naturally less likely to conceive. Long breastfeeding spaces births further apart and lowers lifetime totals without deliberate contraception.
- **phenomena:** PM, FDT
- **seminal:** Bongaarts 1978, Bongaarts and Potter 1983, Konner and Worthman 1980, Wood 1994
- **cross-ref:** D (norms around wet-nursing and formula adoption)
- **notes:** Proximate: raises the question of why breastfeeding duration has changed. Historically both breastfeeding and reproduction are energetically intense and compete; in the modern world energy is not the binding constraint, though the hormonal switch persists. Core proximate determinant in Bongaarts framework.

### A.14. Coital Frequency and Fecundability
- **slug:** `coital-frequency-biological`
- **claim:** Variation in coital frequency — driven by health, age, cohabitation patterns, spousal separation, and customary abstinence — alters monthly fecundability and realized fertility independently of contraceptive use.
- **why:** Monthly probability of pregnancy scales with coital frequency. Anything that lowers frequency — labor migration, war, illness, declining libido — mechanically reduces births even without contraception.
- **phenomena:** PM, FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Bongaarts 1978, Wood 1989, Davis and Blake 1956, Caldwell and Caldwell 1977
- **cross-ref:** D (customary abstinence norms); C (labor migration as driver of spousal separation)
- **notes:** Proximate: just begs the question of why people are having sex less often. Declining coital frequency invoked in recent Japan/Korea/US discussions. Bongaarts proximate determinant.

### A.15. Maternal Age and Fecundity Decline
- **slug:** `maternal-age-fecundity-decline`
- **claim:** Postponement of childbearing into the 30s and 40s reduces realized fertility because fecundity declines and miscarriage risk rises sharply with maternal age, producing unintended childlessness and lower completed fertility.
- **why:** A woman's monthly chance of conceiving falls steeply after her early 30s. Couples who try to have children later end up with fewer than intended; some intended births simply never happen.
- **phenomena:** SDT
- **seminal:** Menken 1985, Leridon 2004, te Velde and Pearson 2002, Schmidt et al. 2012
- **cross-ref:** B (biological fecundity decline with age); C.2.e (Tempo Effects — economic driver of postponement)
- **notes:** Proximate: raises the question of why maternal age at first birth is rising. Converts behavioral postponement into permanent fertility loss.

### A.16. Paternal Age and Sperm Quality Decline
- **slug:** `paternal-age-sperm-quality`
- **claim:** Rising paternal age and declining sperm count/quality reduce couple fecundity and raise time-to-pregnancy, contributing to lower realized fertility in the SDT.
- **why:** Sperm count, motility, and DNA quality decline with male age. Lower sperm quality lengthens time to conception and raises the chance couples give up before succeeding.
- **phenomena:** SDT
- **seminal:** Carlsen et al. 1992, Levine et al. 2017, Sharpe 2010
- **cross-ref:** B.2 (Endocrine Disruptors — one potential driver of declining sperm quality)
- **notes:** Proximate: raises the question of why paternal age is increasing and why sperm quality may be declining. Sperm-count trend disputed methodologically; quantitative contribution to TFR likely small.

### A.17. Assisted Reproductive Technology Access
- **slug:** `art-access-fertility-recovery`
- **claim:** Availability and affordability of ART (IVF, ICSI, egg freezing) partially offsets age-related fecundity decline and enables completion of postponed fertility, raising TFR relative to the counterfactual without ART.
- **why:** ART lets couples who postponed childbearing or face fecundity problems produce a birth they otherwise could not. Realized fertility ends up closer to desired fertility.
- **phenomena:** SDT
- **seminal:** Leridon 2004, Habbema et al. 2009, Sobotka et al. 2008
- **cross-ref:** A.15 (Maternal Age); C (access/cost)
- **notes:** Proximate: bridges gap between desired and realized fertility, analogous to contraception. Does not explain why desired fertility changed. Quantitative contribution small (<5% of births in most OECD) but growing.

### A.18. Genetic and Heritable Variation in Fertility
- **slug:** `heritability-fertility-genetic`
- **claim:** Heritable variation in fertility-related traits explains a portion of between-individual fertility differences and may produce a partial fertility rebound over generations as selection favors high-fertility genotypes.
- **why:** Twin and family studies show that traits influencing family size are partly inherited. If low-fertility behavior reduces fitness, natural selection should over generations favor genes associated with higher fertility.
- **phenomena:** SDT
- **seminal:** Kohler et al. 1999, Kohler et al. 2002, Tropf et al. 2015
- **cross-ref:** D.1.c (Cultural Evolution and Maladaptive Low Fertility — the selection pressure this responds to)
- **notes:** Proximate/mechanism: drives differential population growth but does not explain why variability exists. Macro implication speculative; operates over centuries, not decades.

### A.19. Intergenerational Transmission of Fertility Preferences
- **slug:** `intergenerational-transmission-fertility`
- **claim:** Fertility preferences and family-size norms are transmitted vertically from parents to children, producing persistence and slow adjustment of fertility across generations.
- **why:** Children absorb their parents' attitudes about ideal family size and gender roles, and tend to reproduce those patterns when they become adults. This makes fertility sticky — a society with high-fertility grandparents takes generations to fully adjust.
- **phenomena:** PM, FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Murphy 1999, Murphy and Knudsen 2002, Kolk 2014, Fernandez and Fogli 2009
- **cross-ref:** D (all cultural root-cause entries — transmission preserves whatever norms are current)
- **notes:** Proximate/mechanism: explains persistence and slow convergence, not the direction of change. Epidemiological approach (Fernandez-Fogli) uses immigrants to isolate cultural from environmental effects.

### A.20. Cultural Diffusion Mechanisms
- **slug:** `cultural-diffusion-mechanisms`
- **claim:** The pace and geography of fertility change is shaped by the channels through which cultural norms and information spread — social networks, mass media (radio, TV, soap operas), and linguistic/cultural community boundaries — independent of the content of those norms.
- **why:** Once a cultural norm about family size shifts, how fast and where it spreads depends on the architecture of social ties, the reach of media, and the cohesion of cultural communities. These channels explain timing and spatial clustering of transitions more than their direction or cause.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Coale and Watkins 1986, Bongaarts and Watkins 1996, La Ferrara Chong Duryea 2012, Jensen and Oster 2009, Kohler Behrman Watkins 2001, Watkins 1991
- **cross-ref:** D (all entries — these are mechanisms of spread, not root causes)
- **notes:** Proximate/mechanism: shows how culture spreads but not what underlying cultural value changed or why. Absorbs: `social-network-peer-effects`, `ideational-diffusion-mass-media`, `linguistic-cultural-boundaries-princeton`. Brazil/India quasi-experimental evidence on TV is strong micro-causal for media channel.

## B. Biological

### B.1. Evolutionary Sex Drive and Contraceptive Decoupling
- **slug:** `evolutionary-sex-drive-contraceptive-decoupling`
- **claim:** Evolution wired humans to seek sex, not children; contraception exploits this by severing the link between sex and reproduction, enabling sex-seeking behavior to continue without fertility consequences — a distinct biological root cause of fertility decline that does not require any change in the preference for children.
- **why:** Natural selection shaped human sex drive to produce reproduction, but the proximate desire is for sex, not offspring. This biological design feature creates a structural vulnerability: any technology that lets people have sex without conceiving will reduce fertility even if no one's preference for children has changed. Contraception is that technology.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Dawkins 1976, Trivers 1972, Buss 1994, Hrdy 1999
- **cross-ref:** A.2 (Modern Contraceptive Technology — the enabling mechanism); D.1.a (Postmaterialism — the cultural context that normalizes contraceptive use)
- **notes:** Distinct from proximate entry A.2, which asks "given that people want fewer children, how does contraception help?" This entry asks: "why does contraception so effectively depress fertility?" The answer is that biology never wired the sex drive to require reproductive intent. New entry, not in v1/v2.

### B.2. Endocrine Disruptors and Environmental Toxins
- **slug:** `endocrine-disruptors-environmental-toxins`
- **claim:** Exposure to endocrine-disrupting chemicals (phthalates, BPA, pesticides) and other environmental pollutants impairs male and female reproductive function, lowering fecundity and contributing to SDT-era fertility decline.
- **why:** Industrial chemicals mimic or block human reproductive hormones, lowering sperm quality, disrupting ovulation, and impairing implantation. If a growing share of the population is biologically less able to conceive, realized fertility falls even when desired fertility does not.
- **phenomena:** SDT
- **seminal:** Colborn et al. 1996, Skakkebaek et al. 2016, Swan et al. 2021
- **cross-ref:** --
- **notes:** Root cause: environmental contamination impairs fecundity directly. Mechanistically plausible at individual level; macro contribution to TFR highly contested. One proposed driver of the paternal sperm quality trend (A.16).

### B.3. Infectious Disease and Sterility
- **slug:** `infectious-disease-sterility`
- **claim:** Sexually transmitted and other infections (gonorrhea, chlamydia, TB, malaria, schistosomiasis) reduce fecundity through tubal damage, fetal loss, and amenorrhea; disease eradication raised fertility in mid-20th century Africa and Asia.
- **why:** STIs scar fallopian tubes; chronic infections damage general reproductive function, leaving many women unable to conceive or carry to term. When antibiotics and public health clear these infections, fertility rebounds without any change in desires.
- **phenomena:** PM, FDT
- **seminal:** Romaniuk 1968, Romaniuk 1980, Caldwell and Caldwell 1983, Frank 1983
- **cross-ref:** --
- **notes:** Central to explaining sub-Saharan infertility belt and post-1950 fertility rebound; minor for Western FDT/SDT.

### B.4. Obesity and Metabolic Subfecundity
- **slug:** `obesity-metabolic-subfecundity`
- **claim:** Rising obesity and metabolic syndrome impair ovulation, sperm quality, and pregnancy outcomes, reducing realized fertility in late-SDT populations.
- **why:** Severe obesity and metabolic disorders disrupt the hormonal signals that drive ovulation and sperm production, and raise the rate of pregnancy complications. As obesity prevalence climbs, a growing share of couples take longer to conceive or fail to do so.
- **phenomena:** SDT
- **seminal:** Pasquali et al. 2007, Jensen et al. 2004, Practice Committee ASRM 2015
- **cross-ref:** --
- **notes:** Individual-level effects well-documented; aggregate TFR contribution likely modest.

### B.5. Fetal Loss and Intrauterine Mortality
- **slug:** `fetal-loss-intrauterine-mortality`
- **claim:** Variation in spontaneous abortion and stillbirth rates, driven by maternal health, infection, and nutrition, mechanically alters live-birth fertility across populations and time.
- **why:** Not every pregnancy ends in a live birth. Populations with worse maternal conditions lose more pregnancies and record fewer births per woman even at the same conception rate. Improving maternal health raises the share of conceptions that survive to delivery.
- **phenomena:** PM, FDT
- **seminal:** Bongaarts and Potter 1983, Wood 1994, Casterline 1989
- **cross-ref:** A.1 (Child Mortality Decline — related mortality channel)
- **notes:** Root cause: biological/health conditions determine how many conceptions survive. The behavioral response to child mortality (insurance/replacement motives) is a separate proximate cause (A.1).

## C. Economic

### C.1. Income

#### C.1.a. Income Effect on Fertility
- **slug:** `income-effect-normal-good`
- **claim:** Holding prices and time costs constant, higher household income should raise desired fertility because children are a normal good. The secular failure of this prediction — fertility falls as incomes rise — is the central puzzle the other economic hypotheses address.
- **why:** If children are like other goods people enjoy, richer households should want more of them. This is the baseline prediction; reality contradicts it. Its value is as the counterfactual against which all other economic explanations are calibrated.
- **phenomena:** PM, FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Malthus 1798, Becker 1960, Jones Schoonbroodt and Tertilt 2011, Clark 2007
- **cross-ref:** --
- **notes:** Clark 2007 shows pre-industrial English data where richer households did have more children — income effect dominating before structural change. The FDT/SDT era requires cost and value channels (C.2, C.3) to dominate.

### C.2. Cost of Children

#### C.2.a. Childcare Cost and Availability
- **slug:** `childcare-availability-cost`
- **claim:** Lower-cost or higher-quality formal childcare reduces the opportunity cost of working mothers' fertility, raising completed fertility; absence of affordable childcare operates in reverse.
- **why:** When subsidized daycare is widely available, mothers can keep earning instead of stepping out of the labor force, so an extra child no longer means giving up a career. That lowers the effective price of children and raises fertility.
- **phenomena:** SDT
- **seminal:** Blau and Robins 1989, Del Boca 2002, Bauernschuster Hener and Rainer 2016, Olivetti and Petrongolo 2017
- **cross-ref:** --
- **notes:** Closely tied to female-wage channel; positive effects most evident in continental Europe.

#### C.2.b. Rising Direct Costs of Children
- **slug:** `child-cost-direct`
- **claim:** Rising direct expenditures required per child (schooling, health, consumption norms) reduce fertility by raising the price of children.
- **why:** Modern children require more out-of-pocket spending — tuition, healthcare, enrichment activities, all the things social norms say a proper child needs. As these outlays rise, the budget supports fewer kids at the standard parents feel obligated to meet.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Caldwell 1976, Lino 2017, Folbre 2008, Doepke and Kindermann 2019
- **cross-ref:** D.2.d (Child-Centered Intensive Parenting — norm that drives what proper childcare requires)
- **notes:** Separating direct cost from quality-investment cost (C.3.d) is an identification challenge.

#### C.2.c. Housing Costs and Space Constraints
- **slug:** `housing-costs`
- **claim:** Higher house prices and rents raise the cost of the space input to child-rearing, reducing fertility.
- **why:** Kids need physical space, so a bigger family usually means a bigger home. When rents spiral upward, the implicit price of an extra child rises sharply.
- **phenomena:** SDT
- **seminal:** Mulder and Billari 2010, Dettling and Kearney 2014, Lovenheim and Mumford 2013, Daysal Lovenheim and Wasser 2021
- **cross-ref:** --
- **notes:** Ambiguous sign because home-equity wealth effect partially offsets cost effect.

#### C.2.d. Tax and Transfer Pronatal Policies
- **slug:** `tax-and-transfer-pronatalism`
- **claim:** Child allowances, parental leave, childcare subsidies, and tax credits raise fertility by lowering the net cost of children.
- **why:** Government transfers lower the net price of children. In practice effects are usually small and often shift timing rather than total number.
- **phenomena:** SDT
- **seminal:** Gauthier and Hatzius 1997, Milligan 2005, Laroque and Salanié 2014, Cohen Dehejia and Romanov 2013
- **cross-ref:** --
- **notes:** Effect sizes generally small; tempo vs. quantum debate. Government policies that reduce the *value* of children (state pensions) belong under C.3.c.

#### C.2.e. Female Wage and Opportunity Cost of Time
- **slug:** `female-wage-opportunity-cost`
- **claim:** Rising female wages raise the opportunity cost of child-rearing time, increasing the effective price of children through the substitution effect and reducing fertility.
- **why:** Raising children takes time, predominantly the mother's. As women's wages rise, every hour spent on childcare means more forgone earnings — the substitution effect raises the price of children and dominates the income effect so net fertility falls. Trade-induced increases in female labor demand are one driver of this wage shift.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Mincer 1963, Becker 1965, Willis 1973, Butz and Ward 1979, Schultz 1985, Autor Dorn Hanson Pettersson and Song 2019
- **cross-ref:** D.2.a (Female Empowerment — ideational/autonomy channel for the same phenomenon)
- **notes:** The substitution effect of rising income operating through time costs. Distinct from pure income effect (C.1.a). Trade and globalization folded in here as a driver of the female wage shift.

#### C.2.f. Inequality and Status Competition in Child Investment
- **slug:** `rising-inequality-and-status-competition`
- **claim:** Rising income inequality raises the required quality investment per child to maintain relative status, increasing the effective cost per child and lowering fertility.
- **why:** When inequality is high, the gap between succeeding and falling behind grows, and parents feel compelled to pour more resources into each child to keep them competitive. Raising one child to the new higher standard becomes so expensive that families can afford only a few.
- **phenomena:** SDT
- **seminal:** de la Croix and Doepke 2003, Kearney and Levine 2014, Doepke Hannusch Kindermann and Tertilt 2022
- **cross-ref:** D.2.d (Child-Centered Intensive Parenting — the norm driving competition)
- **notes:** The status competition norm is Cultural; the mechanism through which it reduces fertility is cost. Easterlin relative-income cycling is filed separately under C.6.

#### C.2.g. Urbanization and Rural-Urban Residential Shift
- **slug:** `urbanization-residential-shift`
- **claim:** Migration from high-fertility rural to lower-fertility urban environments lowers aggregate TFR through composition effects, raises the cost of children (housing, reduced kin support), and devalues child labor in urban production.
- **why:** Cities and farms call for different family sizes. On a farm, children help young and space is cheap; in a city, kids contribute little economically, apartments are expensive, and grandparents are far away. Both the composition shift and the changed economics pull fertility down.
- **phenomena:** PM, FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Notestein 1945, Davis 1963, Galor 2011, de la Croix and Doepke 2003
- **cross-ref:** C.3 (child labor devalued in urban production — value channel); D (cultural change associated with urban environments)
- **notes:** Primary home: Cost of Children (housing, reduced informal childcare). Secondary: Value of Children (child labor devaluation). Composition vs. behavioral effects must be decomposed; urban TFR < rural TFR is robust cross-nationally.

### C.3. Value of Children

#### C.3.a. Mode of Production and Child Economic Value
- **slug:** `agricultural-mode-of-production`
- **claim:** Differences in production technology across hunter-gatherer, pastoralist, and agriculturalist economies generate different optimal fertility through variation in child labor value and dependency length.
- **why:** In hunter-gatherer, pastoralist, and farming economies, children produce different amounts of useful labor and become economically self-sufficient at different ages. Where kids contribute early and cheaply, parents gain more from having many.
- **phenomena:** PM
- **seminal:** Boserup 1965, Kaplan 1994, Sellen and Mace 1997, Gibson and Mace 2006
- **cross-ref:** D (norms around child roles vary by production system)
- **notes:** Key for explaining PM cross-population variation. Distinct from C.3.d (QQ): Mode of Production explains baseline fertility levels across production systems; QQ explains the *decline* within industrializing economies as returns to human capital rise.

#### C.3.b. Child Labor Restrictions and Compulsory Schooling
- **slug:** `child-labor-laws-and-schooling`
- **claim:** Legal restrictions on child labor and compulsory schooling lower the economic return to children by eliminating their labor contribution and converting them into long-running expenses, reducing fertility.
- **why:** When governments ban child labor and require school attendance, children stop being a source of family income. The economic calculation flips: an extra child no longer pays their own way.
- **phenomena:** FDT
- **seminal:** Doepke 2004, Doepke and Zilibotti 2005, Hazan and Berdugo 2002, Galor and Moav 2006
- **cross-ref:** --
- **notes:** Primarily about declining value (children stop contributing labor). Tightly linked to C.3.d (QQ): same children now require quality investment instead of providing labor. Policy-driven variation provides identification.

#### C.3.c. Old-Age Security and Pension Crowdout
- **slug:** `old-age-security-pension-crowdout`
- **claim:** Public pensions and formal old-age insurance substitute for children as a retirement-support asset, reducing fertility by eliminating the insurance/investment value of children.
- **why:** Without formal retirement systems, adult children are how parents survive old age — a strong motive for high fertility. When governments provide pensions, that motive evaporates.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Neher 1971, Nugent 1985, Cigno and Rosati 1996, Boldrin Jones and Khitatrakun 2005, Billari and Galasso 2009
- **cross-ref:** A.1 (Child Mortality Decline — related insurance channel)
- **notes:** Causal identification difficult; natural experiments (pension expansions) provide some support. Economic complement to the cultural shift in D.1.b (Caldwell/Westernization).

#### C.3.d. Quantity-Quality Tradeoff
- **slug:** `quantity-quality-tradeoff`
- **claim:** Rising returns to child human capital raise the shadow price of an additional child relative to investing more in each existing child, lowering desired fertility as parents substitute quality for quantity; skill-biased technical change is the labor-market force driving this reallocation.
- **why:** As returns to education rise, parents shift spending toward fewer, more-invested children rather than many less-invested ones. The marginal value of each child's quality rises faster than the value of an extra quantity child, so equilibrium family size falls.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Becker 1960, Becker and Lewis 1973, Becker and Tomes 1976, Galor and Weil 2000, Doepke 2004, Galor and Moav 2002, Greenwood Seshadri and Vandenbroucke 2005
- **cross-ref:** --
- **notes:** Absorbs `skill-biased-technical-change`: SBTC is the technology shock that raises the return to human capital, which drives QQ reallocation — two framings of the same mechanism. Micro evidence mixed (Black-Devereux-Salvanes 2005 finds little tradeoff); macro role central in unified growth theory.

#### C.3.e. Credit Constraints and Liquidity
- **slug:** `credit-constraints`
- **claim:** Imperfect credit markets alter the value of children: in settings without formal finance, children serve as savings/insurance vehicles (raising their value and fertility); in richer settings, liquidity constraints on young households delay and reduce childbearing.
- **why:** Without good banks or insurance, children act as a savings account and old-age safety net, raising their value. In richer settings, young couples cannot borrow against future earnings to fund a family early, so they delay — sometimes permanently.
- **phenomena:** PM, FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Becker 1981, Lehrer and Nerlove 1986, Lovenheim and Mumford 2013
- **cross-ref:** --
- **notes:** Sign ambiguous: insurance motive raises the value of children in poor settings; liquidity constraint reduces effective demand in young SDT households.

#### C.3.f. Intergenerational Wealth Flows Reversal
- **slug:** `wealth-flows-reversal`
- **claim:** Modernization reverses the net direction of intergenerational transfers from child-to-parent to parent-to-child, eliminating the economic rationale for high fertility by turning children from net assets into net liabilities.
- **why:** In traditional economies, children work and support elderly parents — resources flow upward. Modernization flips the arrow: parents subsidize children for decades through schooling. Once children are net cost centers rather than income sources, the economic logic for having many disappears.
- **phenomena:** FDT
- **seminal:** Caldwell 1976, Caldwell 1982, Kaplan 1994, Lee 2000
- **cross-ref:** D.1.b (Cultural Westernization — the cultural channel through which this shift occurs)
- **notes:** This entry captures the *economic mechanism* of Caldwell's theory. The *cultural channel* — that mass schooling and Western ideals drove the reversal — lives in D.1.b. Both entries are needed; they are complementary not redundant.

### C.4. Resource Constraints (Malthusian)

#### C.4.a. Land and Resource Constraints (Malthusian)
- **slug:** `land-and-resource-constraints-malthusian`
- **claim:** In pre-modern settings, fixed land and diminishing returns generate a positive check linking real wages and fertility/mortality, producing a self-correcting demographic equilibrium.
- **why:** In a pre-modern economy, output depends on fixed land, so more people means less food per person. When wages fall below subsistence, people delay marriage and starvation rises, pulling population back down. Fertility moves with real wages because couples adjust marriage timing to economic conditions.
- **phenomena:** PM
- **seminal:** Malthus 1798, Lee 1980, Clark 2007, Ashraf and Galor 2011
- **cross-ref:** A.7 (Age at Marriage — the primary behavioral channel of the preventive check)
- **notes:** Foundation for PM variation. Distinct from C.2 (price per child) and C.3 (what children are worth). This is about the economy-wide resource constraint that sets the real wage. Preventive vs. positive check distinction matters.

### C.5. Economic Uncertainty

#### C.5.a. Economic Uncertainty and Labor-Market Insecurity
- **slug:** `economic-uncertainty-and-unemployment`
- **claim:** Higher job insecurity, unemployment risk, and temporary contracts induce postponement and reduction of fertility.
- **why:** Having a child is a long-term irreversible commitment, so couples want income confidence before taking it on. When jobs are precarious or recessions hit, people postpone childbearing — and enough postponement becomes permanent reduction because biology and life plans do not wait.
- **phenomena:** SDT
- **seminal:** Adsera 2004, Adsera 2011, Sobotka Skirbekk and Philipov 2011, Schneider 2015
- **cross-ref:** --
- **notes:** Salient explanation for post-2008 fertility decline and Southern European lowest-low fertility.

### C.6. Easterlin Cycling

#### C.6.a. Easterlin Relative Income / Cohort Size
- **slug:** `easterlin-relative-income`
- **claim:** Fertility rises when a cohort's earnings prospects are favorable relative to the consumption aspirations formed in their parents' household, generating endogenous fertility cycles driven by cohort size.
- **why:** People judge economic prospects by comparing earnings to the lifestyle they grew up in. A small generation entering a strong labor market feels rich relative to that benchmark and has more kids; a large generation crowding into a weaker market feels poor and has fewer. This produces fertility cycles, not a secular trend.
- **phenomena:** SDT
- **seminal:** Easterlin 1961, Easterlin 1976, Macunovich 1998
- **cross-ref:** --
- **notes:** Distinct from C.1.a (income effect) because it is about relative, not absolute, income and predicts oscillation, not a secular decline. Originally proposed to explain the US baby boom/bust; weaker post-1980 empirical support.

### C.7. Marriage Market

#### C.7.a. Marriage Market and Assortative Mating
- **slug:** `marriage-market-economics`
- **claim:** Changes in marriage market matching (education gaps, search frictions, sex ratios) alter union formation and the cost and productivity of childrearing, reducing fertility where partnership formation is delayed or forgone.
- **why:** Fertility mostly happens inside partnerships, so anything that makes it harder to form or sustain a match reduces births. Mismatches in education, longer search times, or skewed sex ratios delay partnership. Marriage can be thought of as a production complement to childrearing — its decline raises the effective cost of producing well-raised children.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Becker 1973, Wilson 1987, Schwartz and Mare 2005, Greenwood Guner Kocharkov and Santos 2014
- **cross-ref:** A.7 (Age at Marriage); A.10 (Sex Ratio Imbalance); D.2.b (Marriage and Family Norms)
- **notes:** Marriage as a production complement to childrearing — its decline raises the cost of childrearing rather than directly changing the value of children.

## D. Cultural

### D.1. Values, Ideology, and Ideational Change

#### D.1.a. Postmaterialism, Individualism, and Secularization
- **slug:** `postmaterialism-individualism-secularization`
- **claim:** A secular cultural shift from materialist, conformist, and religiously grounded values toward postmaterialist values centered on self-actualization, autonomy, and individual choice reduces desired fertility by redefining the good life away from family formation, removing normative pressure to have children, and weakening religious prescriptions against small families and contraception.
- **why:** Postwar affluence freed people from survival concerns, and values shifted toward self-expression and personal fulfillment. In this value system, children compete with adult goals — career, travel, self-discovery, voluntary childlessness — and the normative obligation to reproduce fades as religious authority declines. Consumerism and aspirational lifestyles reinforce this by elevating non-child consumption as the marker of success.
- **phenomena:** SDT
- **seminal:** Lesthaeghe 1983, van de Kaa 1987, Lesthaeghe and van de Kaa 1986, Lesthaeghe and Surkyn 1988, Inglehart 1977, Norris and Inglehart 2004, Frejka and Westoff 2008, Hagestad and Call 2007
- **cross-ref:** C (consumerism competes with children for budgets); B.1 (Evolutionary Sex Drive — postmaterialism normalizes contraceptive use)
- **notes:** Absorbs: `secular-ideational-shift`, `individualism-rise`, `secularization-religiosity-decline`, `childlessness-as-acceptable-choice`, `consumerism-aspirational-lifestyles`. Canonical SDT framework. Criticized as descriptive rather than causal and hard to separate from income/security mechanisms.

#### D.1.b. Cultural Westernization and Developmental Idealism
- **slug:** `caldwell-wealth-flows-westernization`
- **claim:** Global diffusion of a developmental idealism package — equating modernity with nuclear families, gender equality, late marriage, high child investment, and low fertility — causes fertility decline independently of structural economic change by transmitting Western family ideals through mass schooling and media.
- **why:** A bundle of beliefs about what it means to be modern has spread globally through schools, development institutions, and media. Once absorbed, these ideals reshape family aspirations regardless of whether local economics have actually modernized.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Caldwell 1980, Caldwell 1982, Thornton 2001, Thornton 2005
- **cross-ref:** C.3.f (Intergenerational Wealth Flows Reversal — the economic mechanism this cultural shift produces)
- **notes:** The economic mechanism of Caldwell's theory (wealth flows reversing as children become net consumers) is filed under C.3.f. This entry captures the cultural channel. Distinct from D.1.a (which is internal value change in already-modernized societies) — this entry is about external cultural transmission to societies mid-transition.

#### D.1.c. Cultural Evolution and Maladaptive Low Fertility
- **slug:** `cultural-evolution-demographic-transition`
- **claim:** Prestige-biased cultural transmission causes low-fertility behaviors of high-status individuals to spread through the population, producing sub-replacement fertility despite reduced biological fitness.
- **why:** People imitate high-status individuals: their careers, lifestyles, and family choices. If elites and educated urbanites have fewer children, that low-fertility pattern spreads downward even though copying it reduces the imitators' biological success. Cultural transmission can drive fertility well below what evolution would favor.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Boyd and Richerson 1985, Richerson and Boyd 2005, Newson et al. 2005, Colleran 2016
- **cross-ref:** A.18 (Genetic and Heritable Variation — the selection pressure this creates over generations)
- **notes:** Evolutionary-cultural account of why fertility falls below replacement. Explains the maladaptive character of modern low fertility from a Darwinian perspective.

#### D.1.d. Nationalist and Pronatalist Ideology
- **slug:** `nationalism-pronatalist-ideology`
- **claim:** State-sponsored nationalist and pronatalist ideology raises fertility by normatively framing childbearing as patriotic duty, with modest and mostly transient effects.
- **why:** States sometimes frame having children as a patriotic act through propaganda, awards, and moral rhetoric. When citizens internalize this framing, some who would otherwise stop at fewer children have an additional one. Effects tend to be modest and short-lived because ideology alone does not pay the bills.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Quine 1996, King 1998, Demeny 1986, Gauthier 1996
- **cross-ref:** C.2.d (Tax and Transfer Pronatalism — the economic instrument often paired with this ideology)
- **notes:** Hard to separate ideology from accompanying transfers; mostly small/transient effects in modern data.

### D.2. Gender, Marriage, Family, and Child Norms

#### D.2.a. Female Empowerment and Gender Equity
- **slug:** `female-empowerment-gender-equity`
- **claim:** Cultural shifts toward gender egalitarianism reduce fertility through two channels: female education raises women's aspirations and autonomy over reproductive decisions beyond the wage/opportunity-cost channel; and diffusion of egalitarian gender norms initially lowers fertility when public-sphere equity outruns household equity, partially reversing once equity reaches the household (the "gender revolution" U-curve).
- **why:** Schooling does more than raise wages: it changes how women see themselves and what they think is possible, making them more likely to negotiate for later marriage and contraception. As gender equality expands in the workplace but lags in the home, women bear a punishing double burden, driving fertility very low. Only when household equity catches up do births partially recover.
- **phenomena:** FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Jejeebhoy 1995, Basu 2002, Lutz and Skirbekk 2014, Mason 1987, McDonald 2000, Goldscheider Bernhardt Lappegard 2015, Esping-Andersen and Billari 2015
- **cross-ref:** C.2.e (Female Wage and Opportunity Cost — the economic channel of the same phenomenon)
- **notes:** Absorbs: `female-education-empowerment-norm-channel`, `gender-equity-norms`. The economic channel (rising female wages) is filed under C.2.e; this entry captures the ideational/autonomy/norm channel. Two-stage gender revolution hypothesis tested via U-shape in cross-country TFR-vs-equity data.

#### D.2.b. Marriage and Family Norms
- **slug:** `marriage-family-norms`
- **claim:** Cultural shifts in the normative structure of marriage and family — from universal, early, and obligatory marriage with strict channeling of reproduction through wedlock, toward optional, late, and deinstitutionalized partnership — reduce fertility through delayed union formation, increased partnership instability, and the erosion of pronatalist norms embedded in traditional kinship structure.
- **why:** When marriage was the near-universal gateway to childbearing, its weakening reduces births on net through delayed commitment and less stable partnerships. Traditional kinship structures (joint vs. nuclear, patrilineal vs. bilateral) and honor/shame norms around premarital sex set the pre-transition baseline and determine how early and universally people form unions.
- **phenomena:** PM, FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Hajnal 1965, Hajnal 1982, Cherlin 2004, Lesthaeghe 1995, van de Kaa 1987, Goode 1963, Wrigley and Schofield 1981, Todd 1985, Reher 1998, Perelli-Harris et al. 2010
- **cross-ref:** A.7 (Age at Marriage); C.7.a (Marriage Market)
- **notes:** Absorbs: `deinstitutionalization-of-marriage`, `honor-shame-premarital-sex-norms`, `kinship-systems-family-structure`. Effect on TFR ambiguous in some Nordic settings where nonmarital fertility is high. Hajnal line and European Marriage Pattern central to PM variation.

#### D.2.c. Son Preference and Gender-Biased Fertility Norms
- **slug:** `son-preference-cultural`
- **claim:** Cultural preference for sons sustains higher fertility through continued childbearing until a son is born, with sex-selective abortion partially substituting for the fertility effect in settings where it is available.
- **why:** In cultures where sons carry the family name, inherit, or support parents in old age, couples who only have daughters keep trying for a boy. This mechanically raises fertility. When sex-selective abortion becomes available, the same preference shows up as skewed sex ratios instead of more births, partly defusing the fertility effect.
- **phenomena:** PM, FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Das Gupta 1987, Arnold Choe Roy 1998, Clark 2000, Jayachandran 2017, Ben-Porath and Welch 1976
- **cross-ref:** A.10 (Sex Ratio Imbalance — one consequence); C.3.c (Old-Age Security — one motivation)
- **notes:** Quantitatively large in South/East Asia; eroded by sex-selective abortion in SDT settings.

#### D.2.d. Child-Centered Intensive Parenting Norms
- **slug:** `child-centeredness-intensive-parenting`
- **claim:** Cultural shift toward intensive, child-centered parenting raises the time and emotional cost of each child and reduces desired fertility.
- **why:** When the prevailing norm shifts from children growing up alongside the household to good parenting meaning constant attention, enrichment, and emotional investment, each child becomes vastly more time- and stress-expensive. Parents who internalize this standard feel they can only do it right for a small number of kids.
- **phenomena:** SDT
- **seminal:** Hays 1996, Lareau 2003, Doepke and Zilibotti 2019, Ishizuka 2019
- **cross-ref:** C.2.b (Rising Direct Costs); C.2.f (Inequality and Status Competition — the norm raises cost and feeds status competition)
- **notes:** Cultural norm is the root cause; its effect operates through the economic channel of raised cost per child. Doepke-Zilibotti formalize the link between inequality, intensive parenting norms, and fertility.

## E. Frameworks / Theoretical Scaffolding

*Entries here are modeling frameworks that generate predictions about fertility but are not themselves falsifiable as specific causal mechanisms. They organize the root-cause hypotheses above but should not receive independent GRADE ratings.*

### E.1. Dynastic Altruism (Becker-Barro)
- **slug:** `dynastic-altruism-becker-barro`
- **claim:** Parents choose fertility to maximize dynastic utility under altruism, so changes in the interest rate, mortality, or per-child cost shift fertility via the Euler condition on descendants.
- **why:** Treating parents as choosing how well their whole line of descendants does turns fertility into a normal economic decision shaped by interest rates, child survival, and child costs. Anything that changes these parameters shifts optimal family size.
- **phenomena:** PM, FDT, SDT
- **seminal:** Barro and Becker 1989, Becker and Barro 1988, Alvarez 1999, Jones and Schoonbroodt 2010
- **cross-ref:** C (nests income effects, cost of children, value of children, and QQ within one framework)
- **notes:** Framework rather than testable mechanism; useful for macro calibration and deriving comparative statics across C.1–C.7. Does not generate independent predictions without assuming specific functional forms.
