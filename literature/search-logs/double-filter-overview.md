# How we choose which studies to use — in plain English

We are trying to answer a hard question: does old-age security — pensions and
similar support for the elderly — change how many children people have? To
answer it well, we can't just pick a few studies we happen to know. We have to
look at *everything* anyone has written on the subject, and then carefully whittle
it down to the studies that can actually give us a trustworthy answer.

The problem is scale and mess. There are thousands of papers. Most are not
relevant. Some look relevant but aren't. A few are relevant but don't contain
anything we can measure. And — as we found the hard way — a few "papers" turn out
not to be real at all. So we sort in **two passes**, like sifting a big pile
through two sieves: a coarse one first, then a fine one.

---

## The first pass: the coarse filter — *"cast a wide net"*

The job of the first pass is simple to say and hard to do: **find everything that
might be relevant, and don't lose anything good.** At this stage we would much
rather keep a few things we'll throw out later than accidentally drop a study that
mattered. A missed study is invisible — you never know it's gone — so the whole
design leans toward catching too much rather than too little.

Here is how it works, step by step:

1. **Start from a short list of studies we already trust.** Before searching, we
   hand-build a small set of papers we know are exactly the right kind. Think of it
   as a set of reference samples. Everything else gets measured against them.

2. **Use that trusted list to tune the search.** We try different combinations of
   search words and check how many of our trusted studies each combination manages
   to catch. We keep adjusting until the search reliably finds the studies we
   already know it *should* find. Only then do we trust it to find the ones we
   don't know about.

3. **Follow the trail of references.** Beyond word searches, we also walk the
   citation trail — the studies a paper cites, and the studies that cite it. This
   catches good work that happens to be described in unusual language a word search
   would miss.

4. **Read through everything and sort it.** The search returns a large pile. We
   read through it in stages — a fast first look that keeps anything remotely
   plausible, then a more careful read, and finally a person on our team makes the
   call on the borderline cases. At each step the rule is the same: when in doubt,
   keep it.

What comes out of the first pass is a manageable pile of studies that are **on the
right topic** — but "on topic" is not the same as "usable." That's the second
pass's job.

---

## The second pass: the fine filter — *"keep only what we can measure"*

Now we have a stack of on-topic studies. The second pass tightens the standard.
It does three clean-up jobs, then gets the studies ready to actually use.

1. **Remove the repeats.** The same study often shows up more than once — an early
   draft and the published version, or the same paper listed under two slightly
   different titles. We match these up and keep one copy of each, so we don't
   accidentally count the same result twice.

2. **Check that each study is actually real.** This sounds paranoid, but it
   matters. When we ran the full process for the first time, we discovered that a
   chunk of our reference trail led to papers that **do not exist** — plausible-
   looking titles that no real search can find, essentially made up somewhere
   upstream. So now every study has to be confirmed against public records before
   it counts. Anything we can't confirm is set aside.

3. **Keep only studies that report a number we can use.** A study can be perfectly
   on-topic and still give us nothing to work with — it might tell a story, or
   argue a theory, without ever measuring anything. To be *usable*, a study has to
   report two things: (a) **an actual measured effect** — a number saying how much
   pensions changed childbearing — and (b) **how many people or observations that
   number is based on.** With both, we can weigh it against the others. Without
   them, we set it aside (we don't discard it — it may still inform the discussion,
   just not the calculation).

Alongside those three jobs, the fine filter also does the practical legwork:
**track down the full text** of each study (often the real bottleneck — many are
behind paywalls and have to be pulled from a library), and then **pull out the key
number** from each one.

---

## Why two passes, and why in this order

The two passes pull in opposite directions on purpose.

- The **first pass is generous.** Its worst mistake would be to lose a good study,
  so it keeps too much.
- The **second pass is strict.** Its job is to throw out everything that can't
  carry weight in the final answer — repeats, unreal references, and studies with
  no measurable result.

Doing the generous pass first protects us from quietly losing good evidence. Doing
the strict pass second means we only spend real effort (chasing down paywalled
PDFs, reading closely, pulling numbers) on studies that have already earned it.
Wide net first, fine sieve second.

---

## Where the pension study stands right now

To make it concrete, here is the pension question after both passes:

- The first pass, after all the searching, sorting, and a final human review,
  produced **40 on-topic studies.**
- The second pass has so far confirmed **16 of those 40** carry a usable number
  (a measured effect plus the size of the sample behind it). The rest are either
  waiting on us to obtain the full text, or turned out to be story-only with no
  number to extract.

That 16-study set is what we'll actually build the answer from — and it only exists
because the two passes did their opposite jobs in the right order.
