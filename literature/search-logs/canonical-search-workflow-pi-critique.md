# GACS — PI Critique of the Canonical Search Workflow

**Author:** Anup Malani (PI), with Claude
**Date:** 2026-07-05
**Status:** working draft.

*Responds to Shravan's 2026-07-03 GACS deliverable (`canonical-search-workflow.md`) and its companion evaluation (`canonical-search-workflow-evaluation.md`).*

---

GACS is careful work that answers a question other than the one I asked. The method is soundly designed, its limitations section is the most honest document in the pilot, and its core instruments (a frozen gold set, fold-local term mining, channel-convergence tiering) are contributions the review should keep. What it does not yet establish is that it works. Every headline number comes from a component dry run or a demonstration on the legacy corpus, not from one clean production run, and the gold it measures against is not frozen. The deeper problem is structural: GACS optimizes recall against a gold set that encodes topical relevance, when the pilot's binding constraint is estimand precision. On that test its headline 44 "meta-analysis-ready" studies collapse to ten.

This memo grants the strengths first, at full strength, then lands the concerns, most important first, then says what would make GACS adoptable.

## What GACS is

GACS is a per-hypothesis discovery-to-tiering pipeline. It states a hypothesis as `cause → effect`, splits the query into a mostly-constant fertility "effect axis" AND a swappable "cause axis" of vocabulary clusters, tunes cluster breadth by 10-fold cross-validation against a DOI-keyed gold set, searches by clustered keywords plus a citation snowball, screens through a Haiku → Sonnet → RA cascade, and tiers the survivors by how many channels found each paper. Its yardstick is recall against the gold. Its output is a set it calls "meta-analysis-ready," defined as "(Tier 1 ∪ Tier 2) ∩ the empirical papers that clear the evidence bar."

## What it gets right

**The limitations section is excellent, and it should be read before anything else.** Shravan's §5 is the Shapiro-Gentzkow "honest treatment of limitations" done properly. It found that the OpenAlex snowball tail was roughly 30% junk, and it says so plainly: the pilot's distinct-study count fell "60 → 44" once eight corrupted-title duplicates were merged (one paper, Shen et al. 2020, "appeared under *six* mangled titles") and eight phantom forward-citation records with "no real paper behind them" were dropped. The detective work is real and load-bearing. The tell, a phantom "New Pension in Turkey" study carrying "an **organic-chemistry abstract** mis-joined onto it," is exactly the signature of a hallucinated citation ingested upstream, and diagnosing it correctly is what lets us trust the study counts for the first time. The W-ID rot finding, the pinned DOI-shuffle join bug in `snowball-citations.mjs`, and the harder forward-citation cap are all genuine data-hygiene gains. This is the part of the deliverable I am most confident in.

**The core methodological ideas are sound.** Four of them are worth carrying into every chapter. First, the gold set as an explicit measurement instrument: without a frozen yardstick there is no recall number, no bias diagnostic, and no way to know how much a search missed. Second, fold-local discriminative-term mining, which harvests query terms only from a training fold and measures recall on a held-out fold, so the recall gain is not the circular self-recall that mining and measuring on the same papers would produce. The discipline is stated as a rule: "A new RA must never mine query terms and measure recall on the same papers." Third, Recall(B) against an unbiased, orthogonally-sourced sample, reported as the honest primary figure, with the A−B gap as a vocabulary-bias diagnostic that actually fired on the pilot and signed the bias in the right direction. Fourth, channel-convergence tiering, which keeps the union across channels and surfaces the keyword-unreachable canonical papers the snowball caught (*Children as a Form of Retirement Saving*, *Does Pension Privatization Increase Fertility?*) instead of burying them under an intersection. DOI-keying throughout ties it together. None of these is decoration; each earns its place.

**The attribution is gracious and, as far as I can tell, accurate.** The synthesis names my end-to-end pipeline as "the load-bearing skeleton," credits Alexandra's clustering and budget allocation, and files the snowball and measurement legs under Shravan's own contribution without overstating any of them. Credit is distributed generously and correctly.

## Where it falls short

**1. GACS optimizes the wrong objective.** This is the deep one, and it is the point on which the whole deliverable turns.

The gold set answers a search question: is this a real pension-and-fertility paper? The chapter asks a different question: does this paper identify the effect the chapter is about? My independent review of the same vetted evidence put it this way. "GACS reports 44 'meta-analysis-ready' studies, but that number answers a search question: did the query find pension-and-fertility papers? It does not answer the review's question: do those papers identify the effect the chapter is about. Apply the estimand and three in four fall away." The RA inclusion pass bears this out exactly: of 40 DOI-resolved studies, 10 were marked RETRIEVE and 30 EXCLUDE, and the primary causal cell holds nine. The 44 becomes ten.

The sharpest version of the problem is inside the gold set itself. Of the 14 frozen gold anchors the query is built to recover, seven do not belong in the primary causal cell, and the RA review flagged each. Four fail on outcome: Yuan (child schooling), Neve-Fink (parental survival), Bratti (maternal labor supply), Duflo (child height) are real pension studies measuring something other than fertility. Two identify a different channel: Eibich-Siedler and Ilciukas run through grandparental childcare, which moves fertility the *other* way — real, and as it turns out central to the Second Transition, but opposite in sign, so they cannot share a pooled estimate with the old-age-security motive. One is reverse-direction: Ruthbah instruments fertility to test the mechanism, not the effect. So the instrument that defines search success points, for more than half its anchors, at studies that belong in some other cell than the primary one. A method tuned to maximize recall against that instrument is tuned, in part, to score points for retrieving papers the primary estimand set does not want. GACS is solving for coverage of a topic when the binding constraint is precision about an estimand. As my review put it, "the scarce resource in this pilot was never more papers. It was a sharp definition of what we are trying to measure."

The fix is cheap, because the machinery is already present. Sonnet already emits, per §D2b, an "evidence type, identification strategy, mechanism/cluster tag, and a relevance score." Fold an estimand-and-mechanism gate into what "meta-analysis-ready" means, and measure recall against an estimand-filtered gold rather than a topical one. Until that happens, the 72% recall is recall toward the wrong set.

**2. It answered a different question than the one assigned.** The assignment was a three-way comparison. Each of the three of us ran an independent search and delivered roughly 85 Tier-1 papers, and Shravan's job was to race the three algorithms head-to-head on false-negative rate, false-positive rate, cost, and replicability, then recommend the best single method, an amalgam, or a weighted average. GACS is instead a single synthesized method, framed as the pipeline the three "converged on": "three people, three routes, one spine," so "the task is therefore not to reconcile rival pipelines but to document the one pipeline they all point to." The convergence framing then dissolves the comparison outright: "Commensurability dissolves under unification ... there is nothing to reconcile for future hypotheses."

Convergence may well be real. But it is convenient, and asserting it is not the same as showing the comparison the assignment asked for. The empirical questions remain unanswered. How much did the three delivered Tier-1 sets actually differ, paper for paper? What was each method's false-negative and false-positive rate against an adjudicated inclusion set? We are told the three routes point to one spine; we are not shown the disagreement matrix that would prove it. I want to see it.

**3. It is validated in components, not end to end, so the decision I wanted cannot yet be made.** Shravan says this himself, and I credit the candor: "GACS has been validated in its components, not yet as one end-to-end production run," and "No single run yet goes gold-freeze → tuned query → fresh screen → tiers with the real OpenAlex budget wired in." What exists is a 10-fold CV dry run, a tier-logic demonstration on the legacy corpus, and a Phase-E migration run. What does not exist is one production pass. That means GACS is a design with component demonstrations, not a validated method. And the decision I actually asked for, choose the canonical search algorithm, cannot be made from a design that has not been run once cleanly. You cannot select the best of three methods when the thing on the table is a fourth, synthesized method with no end-to-end result behind it.

**4. Seventy-two percent is both low and soft.** Cochrane-style reviews aim for near-complete recall, and missing roughly 28% of the literature is a lot to concede at the search stage. The number is also soft. It comes from "a **10-fold cross-validation dry run** ... on the *un-frozen* gold set, with *title-only* matching," which the evaluation itself calls a conservative lower bound and warns against quoting: "Reading them as 'GACS recovers 71% of the literature' would over-claim." Two questions stack here, and neither is answered. Against what benchmark is 72% acceptable for a systematic review? And what is the real figure once the gold freezes and abstract matching comes in, given that the number "will move in both directions"?

**5. Circularity is acknowledged but not escaped.** Recall(B) is offered as the honest primary estimate because Tier B is an orthogonal sample. But §5.3 concedes the sample is not actually orthogonal: "The snowball that feeds it was seeded off the (keyword-sourced) PI relevant set. So Recall(B), while the honest primary, still rests on a not-fully-orthogonal sample." Naming a bias is not removing it. The one number the method puts forward as unbiased is, by its author's own account, still biased toward keyword-reachable work. The honest primary figure inherits the circularity it was meant to escape.

**6. The pipeline may be too complex for anyone but its author to run.** GACS is sophisticated: vocabulary-family clustering, 10-fold CV over breadth, fold-local log-odds term mining, channel tiering, an adaptive Haiku/Sonnet cascade, and TF-IDF dual-threshold title matching. Much of it is not yet fit. The open-items list is explicit: "Parameters still to fit: the cross-validation breadth grid; the snowball saturation thresholds ...; the D1 budget cutoff; the Haiku recall-calibration threshold; the cluster-merge overlap threshold; and the bootstrap gold-size floor." The project's stated bar is that a new RA can run the pipeline "without oral tradition." Today the pipeline has several unset knobs and one person who knows how to turn them. That is oral tradition waiting to happen.

**7. The evidence is one hypothesis, and generalization is the property the review needs most.** Everything in the evaluation is old-age security. The method is designed to generalize, but, in Shravan's own words, "generalization is a claim, not yet a result." The cold-start bootstrap, the whole apparatus for a brand-new hypothesis with no baseline corpus to mine the gold from, has never been run. Generalization across the review's many hypotheses is exactly the property we are buying, and it is exactly the one the pilot does not test.

## What would make it adoptable

Five moves, in order, would convert GACS from a promising design into the review's canonical search.

1. **Run the comparison I asked for, even retrospectively.** Take the three delivered Tier-1 sets on the legacy OAS corpus and report, pair by pair, their overlap, each method's false-negative and false-positive rate against the adjudicated inclusion set, its cost, and whether an RA can reproduce it. The data already exist. This is the actual deliverable.
2. **Freeze the gold and do one clean production run.** Adjudicate the 52 Tier-B UNCERTAINs, sign off Tier A, then run gold-freeze → tuned query → fresh screen → tiers once, end to end, with the real budget wired in. Replace the three component stand-ins with a single number chain.
3. **Add an estimand-and-mechanism gate before "meta-analysis-ready," and re-measure.** Gate on Sonnet's mechanism and evidence-type tags so the set counts only Cell-A estimand matches, build an estimand-filtered gold, and report how the 72% moves against it. This is the fix for critique 1, and it is small.
4. **Fit the parameters and write the defaults down.** Set every knob in §7, record a default and a one-line justification for each, and hand the pipeline to a second RA to run without you in the room. If they can, the replicability bar is met.
5. **Replicate on a second hypothesis,** ideally a well-studied one with prior meta-analyses, to exercise the cold-start bootstrap and turn "designed to generalize" into "generalizes."

## Bottom line

GACS is the right architecture aimed at the wrong target, shown in parts but not yet run whole. Keep the instruments. The frozen gold, the fold-local mining, the channel tiering, and the data-hygiene fixes are worth carrying into every chapter. Before GACS becomes the review's canonical search, it needs three things it does not have: the head-to-head comparison the assignment asked for, one clean end-to-end run against a frozen gold, and an estimand gate so that "recovering the literature" means recovering the studies that identify the effect, not the studies that mention the words. The honest label is Shravan's own: validated in components, pending a clean run and a freeze. That is a strong place to be at this stage. It is not yet a decision.
