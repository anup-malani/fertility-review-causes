# Old-Age-Security / Pension Crowd-Out of Fertility — PI Independent Review (first pass)

**Author:** Anup Malani (PI), with Claude
**Date:** 2026-07-05
**Status:** working draft, v4. Corrects the Second-Transition verdict (see the v4 note below).
**Inputs:** GACS `metaanalysis-doi-list.md` (44 distinct ET=4 studies); `ra-review.csv` (40 DOI-resolved
studies with RA RETRIEVE/EXCLUDE decisions); the 4 unresolved title-keyed studies, now resolved (§3.5).

Expanding old-age security lowers fertility. Nine clean quasi-experiments say so, in one direction,
with no reversals, so the mechanism is real at the household level. Its reach into the two transitions
this review targets is limited, and the limits differ. State pensions arrived too late to have caused
the First Transition; there, financial-market development is the channel timed right. The Second
Transition runs on two opposing channels, an old-age-security motive that lowers fertility and a
grandparental-childcare channel that raises it, and the cleanest rich-country evidence is the second,
which points the right way as pensions tighten and retirement ages rise. So old-age-security policy is a
plausible, correctly-signed contributor to the Second Transition, though likely a modest one.

This document is the PI's own read of the same vetted evidence the RAs worked from. It fixes an
estimand, re-decides the RAs' inclusion calls, maps the evidence, grades it on four measures, and
applies those measures across the three phenomena. The point is an independent benchmark, reached by
hand, that Alexandra's pipeline chapter can be checked against.

**What changed in v4.** One correction, and it moves a verdict. The earlier draft buried the
grandparental-childcare channel in a secondary cell and then dismissed old-age security for the Second
Transition on a "wrong sign" argument: pensions were cut, the motive predicts fertility should rise, it
fell. That argument only holds for the old-age-security *motive*. The grandparental-childcare channel
runs the other way, and it is the cleaner rich-country evidence. As retirement ages rose and early
pensions were trimmed, grandparents stayed at work, childcare fell, and so did fertility — the right
sign for the Second Transition. So the channel the earlier draft walled off is the one that actually
reaches the Second Transition. v4 promotes it, rewrites the Second-Transition verdict, and flags two
further cracks: China is below replacement for one-child-policy reasons, not Second-Transition dynamics,
so it is weak evidence for that transition; and "no sign reversals" partly reflects what a keyword search
publishes, not only what is true. (Earlier revisions fixed the estimand, built the four-cell structure
and the four-measure framework, and sorted studies by replacement level.)

## 1. What counts as evidence (the estimand)

One question decides which studies belong in this chapter: does the study estimate the effect of
*non-child old-age security* on *fertility*, through the *old-age-security motive*? Most papers that
mention pensions and fertility do not.

The hypothesis is old and specific. Children were once a household's retirement plan, its insurance and
its investment for old age (Neher 1971; Caldwell 1976; Cigno 1993). Give parents another way to secure
old age, whether a state pension, social insurance, long-term-care insurance, or a bank that lets them
save, and the retirement demand for children falls. Fertility follows.

The estimand is therefore the causal effect of an exogenous rise in non-child old-age security on
completed fertility, working through that motive. Three coordinates pin it down, and the whole
adjudication turns on getting them right.

| Coordinate | In | Out |
|---|---|---|
| **Treatment** | Pension introduction or expansion, social security, long-term-care insurance, or financial-market development that lets households save instead of bearing children. | General income shocks (lottery), child grants (the price of children), childcare-supply shocks, welfare family caps, marriage-age laws. These test other hypotheses. |
| **Outcome** | Fertility as the dependent variable: births, completed fertility, parity progression, TFR. | Savings, migration, coresidence, child schooling, elderly health, labor supply. Real pension effects, but a different outcome. See Cell D: some of these trace the causal chain even though fertility is not the outcome. |
| **Direction and mechanism** | Treatment → fertility, through the old-age-security motive. | Reverse causation (fertility → pension take-up) is not the estimand, but it updates the prior on whether children are old-age security. The grandparental-childcare channel is a second, opposite-signed pathway; it is not the OAS motive, but it *is* central to the Second Transition (Cell C). |

The binding constraint here is the estimand, not the search. GACS reports 44 "meta-analysis-ready"
studies, but that number answers a search question: did the query find pension-and-fertility papers? It
does not answer the review's question: do those papers identify the effect the chapter is about. Apply
the estimand and three in four fall away from the primary cell. The scarce resource in this pilot was
never more papers. It was a sharp definition of what we are trying to measure.

## 2. Four cells, not one axis

The RAs sorted each study into RETRIEVE or EXCLUDE. That single axis buries four different objects, and
the difference is not cosmetic: what may be pooled, and what merely informs the prior, depends on which
object a study is.

- **Cell A, primary.** Exogenous non-child old-age security moves fertility, through the OAS motive. The
  causal estimate is built from these and only these.
- **Cell B, mechanism.** Does a child actually function as old-age security? Fertility is the *treatment*
  and an old-age-security asset (private insurance, savings) is the outcome. Evidence that the mechanism
  exists, not an estimate of the fertility effect. Never pooled with A.
- **Cell C, the second channel.** A pension moves fertility through grandparental childcare, not the OAS
  motive, and it moves it the other way: a retired grandparent's free time lowers the cost of a
  grandchild and *raises* the daughter's fertility. Opposite in sign, so never pooled with Cell A. But
  not a curiosity: these are rich, below-replacement countries, so this is the cleanest evidence we have
  for the Second Transition, and its sign is the right one for it (§6).
- **Cell D, indirect channels and chain links.** The treatment is old-age security and the outcome is one
  step short of fertility: private saving, children's schooling or health, coresidence, or old-age
  support itself. These do not estimate the fertility effect, but they light up the links in the causal
  chain the OAS story runs through, and several update the prior on whether the mechanism operates at
  all.

Everything outside these four cells is out of scope.

### Cell C — the grandparental-childcare channel (3): the Second-Transition evidence

| # | Study | Setting | Sign | Ours |
|--:|---|---|---|---|
| 8 | Eibich & Siedler 2020 | Germany | grandparent time ↑ → daughter fertility ↑ | **C** |
| 10 | Ilciukas 2023 | Netherlands | delayed grandparent retirement → fertility ↓ | **C** |
| 19 | Akyol & Atalay 2025 | Australia | delayed grandmother pension → fertility ↓ | **C** |

The RAs were right to keep these out of the Cell A estimate, and wrong to drop them. They are the
cleanest causal evidence in rich, below-replacement settings — Germany, the Netherlands, Australia —
which makes them the cleanest Second-Transition evidence in the whole set. The sign is what matters. A
retired grandparent's time raises a daughter's fertility, so raising retirement ages and trimming early
pensions, which is what the Second-Transition era did, lowers it. That is the right sign for the observed
decline. These three are promoted to the center of the Second-Transition verdict in §6. They are still
never pooled with the opposite-signed Cell A; two channels with opposite signs cannot share an estimate.

### Cell D — indirect channels and chain links (~12, re-screened from "wrong outcome")

A pension study whose outcome is not fertility can still trace a link in the OAS chain, and a link that
itself bears on fertility. Three groups fall out.

**D1. Does the OAS substitute exist? Pension crowds out private saving.** Choukhmane et al. (one-child
policy → household saving), Lehmann-Hasemeyer & Streb (Bismarck insurance → private saving), Lachowska &
Myck (Polish reform → saving). If a pension crowds out the *saving* motive, it is crowding out the same
asset demand the OAS story says it crowds out for *children*. The saving elasticity is a rough ceiling on
the fertility elasticity.

**D2. Do children actually deliver old-age support?** Chen & Fang (fewer children → less old-age
support), Zhang (children → pension take-up), Neve & Fink (children's schooling → parent's survival), and
the coresidence and migration studies (Chen 2015, Eggleston et al., Edmonds et al., Chen et al. 2017, Bau
et al.). These test whether children pay the old-age dividend the motive assumes.

**D3. Quantity-quality channel.** Yuan et al., Mu & Du (pension → children's schooling), Duflo (pension →
child health). If a pension reform raises investment *per* child, that is the quality margin, which
co-moves with lower quantity.

Cell D never enters the fertility estimate. It sets priors, bounds magnitudes, and tells the reader which
links in the chain are independently supported.

### Reverse causation, reconsidered

The reverse-causation studies shift the prior. Chen & Fang (fewer children → less old-age support) is
direct evidence that children *are* old-age security, which is the premise of the whole chapter; it
raises the prior. Zhang (children → pension take-up) cuts the other way, and its sign is the test: if more
children *raise* pension take-up, children and pensions are complements, not substitutes, and the OAS
story weakens; if more children *lower* take-up, they are substitutes, and it strengthens. So the reverse
studies are Bayesian updates whose direction we read off the sign, which means pulling the two estimates
(flagged for retrieval). Both sit in Cell D.

### Excluded — genuinely out of scope (~11)

- **Wrong treatment (not old-age security):** Rosenberg (child grant), Kearney (welfare family cap), Tao
  (kindergarten supply), Bulman (lottery income), Bellés-Obrero-Lombardi (marriage age), Broeck-Maertens
  (female employment), Amarante (cash transfer to pregnant women), Valentová (parental leave).
- **No fertility link even at one remove:** Bérgolo-Cruces (labor informality), Serrano-Alarcón (retiree
  health and labor). Bratti sits at the border: pension → daughter's labor supply is a step toward the
  grandparental-childcare channel, so it is better read alongside Cell C than dropped.

Every credible design points the same way. Expand non-child old-age security and fertility falls;
withdraw it and fertility rises. No study in Cell A reverses that sign. Read the unanimity with one
caution, though: a keyword search recovers *published* pension-fertility studies, and the expected sign
is the publishable one, so the absence of reversals partly reflects what reaches print, not only what is
true. Taken with that caveat, the directional agreement across five countries and 150 years is still the
strongest thing the Cell A evidence has.

Two further channels sit outside the Cell A estimate. Cell B corroborates the mechanism from the reverse
direction: Ci and Ruthbah find that more children means fewer purchased old-age assets, so children and
financial old-age security are substitutes. **Cell C is not a side channel for the Second Transition; it
is the main evidence there**, and §6 treats it so — the grandparental-childcare studies find the opposite
sign to Cell A, which is exactly why the two cannot be pooled and why the net effect of any pension reform
is an empirical question rather than a single number.

## 5. How to grade a study: four measures

The four measures separate two questions a single "how good is this study" rating runs together. Does the
study *identify a causal effect*, and does that cause *explain much of the variation in fertility*? Each
can be asked inside the study's own sample or outside it. That gives a two-by-two.

| | Does it identify a causal effect? | Does it explain much variation? |
|---|---|---|
| **In-sample** | **(a) Credibility.** Strength of the identification. | **(c) Internal R².** Share of in-sample fertility variation the cause moves. |
| **Out-of-sample** | **(b) External validity.** Does the causal estimate transport to other settings? | **(d) Demographic significance.** Does the cause explain the actual fertility decline in the target period? |

The four come apart, and one more thing splits them here: **credibility attaches to a channel, not to
"pensions" in the abstract.** A pension reform runs an OAS-motive channel (−) and a childcare channel (+)
at once, so a study is credible *about one channel*, and the two carry opposite signs. With that in mind,
the OAS literature sits in a clear corner. Its best studies are credible (a). They are modest on in-sample
explanatory power (c), because a pension reform shifts births by a few percent while income, education,
and mortality move the rest. They are limited on transport (b). And on demographic significance (d) they
are modest, low for the motive channel in both transitions and modest-but-real for the childcare channel
in the Second (§6).

Measures (c) and (d) need numbers the abstracts do not carry: an in-sample R² or variance share, and a
decomposition of the target-period decline. Both are flagged for the full-text and macro-data pass. Until
those land, the significance verdicts below are arguments from timing, saturation, and sign — not yet
measurements.

## 6. Verdicts by phenomenon (first pass)

Studies are sorted by the demography, not the calendar or the income level. A country above replacement
fertility at the time of treatment is living an FDT-like decline; a country at or below replacement an
SDT-like one. That test, which the PI proposed, assigns most of the modern studies. It needs one caveat,
below, for China.

### Pre-modern

No study speaks to the pre-modern period, because no pre-modern setting offers exogenous variation in
formal old-age security. Credibility (a) is not assessable, and (b), (c), (d) have nothing to sit on.

The mechanism still matters little for the pre-modern *question*, which is about variation. Before formal
pensions, every household relied on children for old age. A universal condition cannot explain why
fertility differed across pre-modern populations. It explains why the level was high, not why it moved.
The variation has to come from elsewhere: kin-support norms, mortality, land and inheritance.

**Verdict:** no direct evidence; demographic significance low for pre-modern variation.

### First Demographic Transition (~1870–1965)

State pensions cannot have caused the First Demographic Transition. They came too late.

The dates settle it. Marital fertility across northwest Europe began falling in the 1870s and 1880s.
Bismarck's pension insurance dates to 1889, American Social Security to 1935. A cause cannot postdate its
effect, so state old-age security cannot be what set the transition going. At most it deepened a decline
already under way, and even that reach is limited: early coverage was thin, so the population share
actually exposed was small. That is measure (d) coming up short even where (a) is respectable. Six studies
sit here now, counting Ghana and the two Bismarck papers, all pointing one way, on credible but historical
designs.

One channel is timed correctly, and it is the reason the broad reading of old-age security matters. A
family did not need Bismarck to save for old age if a bank would do it. Financial-market development
spread through the nineteenth century, ahead of state pensions, and Basso and Cuberes identify exactly
this effect on fertility in nineteenth-century US counties. So whatever demographic weight (d) the
mechanism carries for the First Transition rests mostly on financial-market substitution, not on public
pensions.

**Verdict:** (a) low-to-moderate; (b) moderate within the historical West; (c) small; (d) partial, and it
runs through financial development rather than state pensions.

### Second Demographic Transition (~1965–present)

The Second Transition runs on two opposing channels, and they must be graded separately, because they
carry opposite signs. The mistake to avoid is grading "pensions" as one thing.

**The old-age-security motive: wrong sign, and saturated.** By the time the Second Transition began, rich
countries already had near-universal pensions, so the fresh expansion the motive needs had already
happened. And the motive predicts the wrong sign for the era: where rich countries trimmed pensions to
cope with aging, less old-age security should mean *more* children, yet fertility fell. Italy
(Billari-Galasso) is the rich-country case. On this channel the mechanism is both saturated and
wrong-signed for the Second Transition, which is the correct reading of the earlier draft's argument — it
just does not generalize to the other channel.

**The grandparental-childcare channel: right sign, and the cleanest evidence.** Eibich-Siedler (Germany),
Ilciukas (Netherlands), and Akyol-Atalay (Australia) are clean natural experiments in rich,
below-replacement countries — the exact setting of the Second Transition. They identify the other
channel: a grandparent's retirement or pension frees time for childcare and raises the daughter's
fertility. Run the era through it and the sign is right. The Second-Transition decades raised retirement
ages and cut early pensions, so grandparents stayed at work, childcare fell, and fertility fell with it.
This is the channel that actually reaches the Second Transition, and it points the right way. It is also
the effect the team flagged as novel.

**So the verdict is a contest, not a dismissal.** Old-age-security and retirement policy is a plausible,
correctly-signed contributor to the Second-Transition decline through the childcare channel, working
against a wrong-signed, saturated motive channel. Which dominates is an empirical question the review
should answer, not assume. Credibility for the childcare channel is moderate — three clean natural
experiments. Its demographic significance is the open magnitude: the Cell C effects are real but not large
(a grandparent's availability shifts fertility by a few percent), so the channel is a modest contributor,
not the primary driver of a decline that also runs on postponement, values, and the cost of children.

**China does not rescue the motive channel for this transition.** China (Shen, Han) is below replacement,
which by the replacement test reads as Second-Transition. But China reached below replacement through the
one-child policy, a coercive quantity restriction, not through Second-Transition postponement dynamics. So
China is better read as evidence that the old-age-security motive can still bite at low fertility where
pensions newly expand — relevant to future low-fertility developing countries — than as clean evidence
about the rich-country Second Transition.

**Verdict:** (a) moderate for the childcare channel, low for the motive channel; (b) the childcare channel
transports within the rich low-fertility world, the China motive evidence does not; (c) small; (d)
plausibly modest and, through the childcare channel, correctly signed — a genuine but minor contributor,
not the non-player the motive channel alone would suggest.

### The frame mostly holds, with one caveat

Sorting by replacement level assigns most studies cleanly: Namibia, Brazil, and Ghana above replacement
and First-Transition-like; the grandparental studies and Italy below replacement and Second-Transition.
The caveat is China. It is below replacement by the test, but below replacement by *policy*, not by the
postponement-and-values dynamics that define the Second Transition. So the replacement test is the right
first cut, and it needs a second question: did the country reach low fertility through Second-Transition
dynamics or through something else? China fails that second test, which is why it is weak Second-Transition
evidence.

### Bottom line

The old-age-security motive is real and well identified at the household level. Across five countries and
150 years, expanding non-child old-age security lowers fertility, in one direction. High marks on
credibility. The reach into the two target transitions is limited, and limited differently in each. For
the First Transition, state pensions came too late, so the weight falls on financial-market development,
which was timed right. For the Second Transition, the motive channel is saturated and wrong-signed, but a
second channel — grandparents' pensions and retirement, working through childcare — is correctly signed
and is the cleanest evidence we have in Second-Transition settings, so old-age-security policy is a genuine
if modest contributor there, not a non-player. The honest one-line summary: the mechanism is causally
credible wherever it is measured, demographically modest, and reaches the Second Transition mainly through
the childcare channel the standard narrative leaves out.

**Grades.** Pre-modern: no direct evidence, low significance. First Transition: low-to-moderate
credibility, partial significance through financial development. Second Transition: moderate credibility
for the grandparental-childcare channel and low for the motive channel; significance modest but correctly
signed through childcare.
