# Chapter 2: Child-Labor Laws and Compulsory Schooling

**Category:** Economic
**Primary mechanism:** Laws that restrict children's work or require school attendance lower the economic return to a child and raise the investment each child needs, so parents choose fewer births.
**Target phenomena:** PM / FDT / SDT (scoped to the First Demographic Transition)
**Cross-references:** Agricultural mode of production (children's productive value without a law, TICK-030); education and fertility (the quantity-quality tradeoff); old-age security (children as investment assets)
**Draft status:** First full draft from the frozen retrieval set. Full-text extraction and quantitative pooling are pending.
**Last updated:** 2026-07-17

Two terms carry precise meaning throughout this chapter. "Quantum" fertility is the number of children a woman bears by the end of her childbearing years: completed fertility, children ever born, cohort parity. "Tempo" is the timing of those births: age at first birth, teenage childbearing. A law can change tempo while leaving quantum fixed. It can push a first birth from age 16 to age 19 and still leave the final family the same size. That distinction sets the limits of what this chapter can conclude.

## 1. The claim

Legal limits on child labor and compulsory-schooling mandates reduce completed fertility. The claimed direction runs from a binding law to fewer births per woman. A binding law is a minimum working age, a factory act, a compulsory-attendance rule, or a school-leaving age that changed how much children worked or attended school. Two economic channels carry the effect. A restriction on child work lowers the income a child is expected to bring in. A schooling requirement raises the human-capital investment each child needs. Both lower the net return to an additional child, so the theory predicts lower quantum fertility. This chapter tests whether that predicted fall shows up in credibly identified evidence, and whether it shows up at the time and place of the First Demographic Transition, roughly 1870 to 1965.

## 2. Theoretical mechanism

The mechanism sits inside the quantity-quality model of fertility. Parents trade the number of children against the resources spent per child. A child who works is partly an asset: the child returns income to the household. A law that bans that work removes the return and turns the child into a pure cost. A law that mandates schooling raises the required spending per child and shifts parents toward fewer, more-educated children. Cigno and Rosati (2005) give the fullest treatment of this child-labor-and-fertility system, and their later work (2024) reviews the evidence that fertility and child labor are chosen jointly.

The joint choice leaves the sign of the fertility response uncertain in theory. Baland and Robinson (2000) build a model where child labor is inefficient yet still arises, and they show the implications of a child-labor ban for fertility are ambiguous. Hazan and Berdugo (2001) and Mookherjee, Prina, and Ray (2012) generate similar joint dynamics, where a policy that raises the return to education can move an economy from a high-fertility, high-child-labor state to a low-fertility, high-schooling state. Greenwood and Seshadri (2004) place the demise of child labor around 1900 inside a broader account of falling fertility and rising skill. The theory is rich, but it does not deliver a single-signed prediction that a child-labor law must lower completed fertility.

The theory also names a channel that few empirical studies test. The hypothesis is about the child as an asset: restrict the child's work, and the return to bearing that child falls. Most identified studies instead measure the effect of the prospective mother's own schooling on her own fertility. Those are different objects. A woman kept in school longer may have fewer or later births for reasons that have nothing to do with the labor value of her future children. The chapter holds the two apart and returns to the gap in Section 6.

## 3. Search strategy

The search followed the project's Gold-Anchored Clustered Search procedure in cold-start mode, meaning it began without a pre-existing labeled set of relevant papers. The cold start began with seven hand-chosen anchor studies spanning the routing problem: direct empirical papers, timing-only papers, theory, and near-miss papers on adjacent topics. These anchors seeded a citation frame, a candidate pool built from two directions: the reference lists of the anchors (backward) and the papers that cite the anchors (forward). The frame comes through a different discovery channel than any keyword query, so that query's blind spots do not shape which papers enter the pool.

The screen sorted every candidate into an estimand cell. An estimand cell is the specific combination of treatment, fertility outcome, and routing that a paper belongs to. The primary cells require both a covered law and a completed-fertility outcome. Separate cells hold timing-only evidence, formal theory, first-stage effects on work or attendance with no fertility outcome, and studies of education or schooling expansion that carry no legal-policy variation. A blinded title-and-abstract rubric assigned each paper to a cell without showing the screener how the paper was discovered or whether it was an anchor. The full rubric, scope, and channel logs sit in `literature/search-logs/child-labor-laws-and-schooling-*`.

## 6. Quantitative synthesis

The evidence splits along the tempo-quantum line, and that split drives the chapter's verdict. The well-identified studies agree that compulsory-schooling laws delay births. They disagree with, or fail to support, the claim that the same laws reduce the number of births.

The tempo effect is consistent, and its mechanism is clear. Black, Devereux, and Salvanes, DeCicca and Krashinsky, Adamecz-Völgyi and Scharle, Wilson, Kırdar and coauthors, and Arendt and coauthors all find that keeping girls in school lowers teenage childbearing and raises age at first birth. Several authors name the same channel: a girl enrolled in school during the term is less exposed to pregnancy, so births drop while she is enrolled and recover afterward. Kırdar and coauthors show the effect vanishing once students are free to leave school, and Wilson shows it fading after age 20. This is an incapacitation channel, meaning school attendance itself blocks births during the school year rather than changing lifetime preferences.

The quantum effect is weak, null, or wrong-signed in the best-identified studies. Geruso and Royer find lower teen fertility and no change in completed fertility by age 45. Cummins, using 1.5 million linked English women and a regression discontinuity, finds zero causal effect on marital fertility. Kan and Lee find that the Taiwanese law raised schooling and left fertility flat. Fort, Schneeweis, and Winter-Ebmer find a negative effect in England but a positive effect on the Continent, where more schooling raised the number of children. Three of the six quantum studies with usable abstracts report a null or positive quantum effect, and none reports a large negative one. A meta-analysis is feasible once effect sizes are extracted, because at least three quantum studies share the completed-fertility outcome. The direction of the current evidence does not support pooling toward a negative completed-fertility effect.

The studies identify a different mechanism from the one the hypothesis names. The hypothesis is about the labor value of the child: ban the child's work, and the return to bearing the child falls. Almost every identified study instead estimates the effect of a woman's own schooling on her own fertility, and reads the result through incapacitation or her own human capital. None isolates the channel where restricting children's work lowers the return to the next child. A reduced-form policy effect can still support the law-to-fertility link, but it does not prove the child-labor-value channel. Pooling waits on full-text extraction, which will record for each study which schooling the law changed and which channel the paper identifies.

## 7. Demographic significance

### 7.1 Pre-modern

No study in the set covers the pre-modern period, and no covered law existed before the nineteenth century. The mechanism cannot explain pre-modern fertility variation because its treatment, a binding child-labor or schooling law, was absent. The productive value of children in subsistence economies matters, but it operates without any law and belongs to the agricultural-mode-of-production hypothesis (TICK-030). Verdict: insufficient data.

### 7.2 First Demographic Transition

The hypothesis is scoped to the FDT, and this is where the evidence is thinnest against the target. Every credibly identified study uses a twentieth-century reform, the earliest being the 1936-1947 English changes, and most fall between 1968 and 2010. The original nineteenth-century factory acts and first compulsory-schooling laws, the ones that coincided with the Western fertility decline, have no quasi-experimental estimate in the set. The credible evidence therefore sits at the tail of the FDT or after it. Even taken at face value, that evidence shows a timing effect and no reliable completed-fertility effect. A law that moves a first birth from 16 to 19 does not lower the number of children, and completed fertility is what the FDT decline measures. Slope sufficiency is insufficient: a null-to-small quantum effect cannot generate the large FDT fall in births. Verdict: not significant for the completed-fertility decline.

### 7.3 Second Demographic Transition

Most of the studies sit in the SDT window, in already-low-fertility settings. The tempo evidence is strong here: compulsory schooling cut teenage childbearing across the United States, Norway, Canada, Turkey, Hungary, Denmark, and the United Kingdom. That result matters for teenage-birth policy. It does not explain the SDT, which is a decline in completed fertility and a shift to later, fewer, or no children. The quantum studies in this window report null (Kan and Lee, Geruso and Royer, Cummins) or positive (Fort and coauthors, Continental Europe) effects. The laws rearranged the timing of births without lowering their number. Verdict: not significant for the completed-fertility decline; the tempo effect is well-identified but answers a narrower question.

## 8. GRADE rating

The rating applies to the hypothesis's causal claim, that covered laws reduce completed fertility. A separate rating covers the narrower tempo claim, where the evidence is stronger.

**Pre-modern.** Very low. No treatment existed and no study covers the period.

**FDT, quantum claim.** Low. The credible studies are all modern, mistimed against the nineteenth-century transition, and they show null or inconsistent completed-fertility effects. One credible quasi-experiment (Cummins) reports a precise zero.

**SDT, quantum claim.** Low. Several well-identified studies converge, but they converge on a null or positive completed-fertility effect, which does not support the claim.

**Tempo sub-claim (FDT and SDT).** Moderate. Multiple regression-discontinuity studies across at least seven countries agree that compulsory schooling delays teenage births through an incapacitation channel. This is credible but narrower than the hypothesis, and it does not move completed fertility.

## 9. Verdict

| | Causal credibility | Demographic significance |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-modern | Very low | Insufficient data |
| FDT | Low (quantum claim); Moderate (tempo sub-claim) | Not significant for the completed-fertility decline |
| SDT | Low (quantum claim); Moderate (tempo sub-claim) | Not significant for the completed-fertility decline |

The one-line reading: child-labor and schooling laws delay births across studies but do not reduce their number, and the credible evidence is too late to bear on the First Demographic Transition it was scoped to explain.

## 10. Open questions and recommended studies

Four gaps would change the verdict if filled.

First, the mechanism gap. No study in the set identifies the child-labor-value channel, the effect of restricting a child's own work on the parent's demand for the next child. A design using a minimum-working-age reform that binds on children's employment, with completed fertility as the outcome, would test the hypothesis on its own terms rather than through the mother's schooling. Second, the timing gap. The nineteenth-century factory acts and early compulsory-attendance laws have staggered adoption across regions and countries, which supports difference-in-differences on historical parish or census data. Such a study would place credible evidence inside the FDT window for the first time. Third, the completed-fertility gap. The tempo studies follow women only through their twenties. Linking the same reforms to cohort completed fertility would show whether delayed teen births are made up later, which the current nulls suggest. Fourth, the extraction gap inside this project. Full-text coding of the 15 frozen studies, recording each paper's identified channel and whose schooling the law changed, is the immediate next step and is required before any pooled estimate.
